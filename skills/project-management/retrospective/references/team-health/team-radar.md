# Team Radar

<Metadata>
Name: Team Radar
Category: team-health
Aliases: Team Radar Chart, Spider Chart Assessment, Competency Radar, Team Maturity Assessment
Duration: 45–60 minutes
Team Size: 4–12
Best For: Multi-dimensional team self-assessment visualized as a radar/spider chart that reveals the team's strengths and improvement areas at a glance
Definition: A multi-dimensional team assessment where members rate the team on various competency and health dimensions, visualized as a radar (spider) chart.
</Metadata>

<Gotchas>
1. Use when: Quarterly or bi-annual cadence for periodic self-assessment; After team formation or significant membership change; When a team needs a thorough honest self-assessment; To track improvement over time with quantitative data; When comparing against a target state or desired team profile
2. Do NOT use when: Every sprint (too time-intensive and diminishing returns); When immediate action items are needed (this is diagnostic); When the team isn't ready for honest self-assessment; As a performance metric imposed by management
3. Too many dimensions: Assessing 15+ dimensions makes scoring superficial and discussion impossible in the timebox. Fix: Maximum 10 dimensions. Drop any that are consistently 8+ and add context-specific ones.
4. Generic dimensions: Using off-the-shelf dimensions without customizing for team context. "Innovation" means different things to different teams. Fix: Write clear definitions and anchors for YOUR context.
5. Using as performance metric: Sharing radar results with management as a team performance score. Fix: Radar data belongs to the team. It's a self-improvement tool, not an evaluation tool.
6. No trend tracking: Running isolated assessments without comparing to previous results. Fix: The value is in trajectory. Keep a simple spreadsheet of scores over time.
7. All dimensions, no focus: Identifying 5 low dimensions and trying to improve all of them. Fix: Pick 1–2 maximum. Demonstrate improvement is possible before expanding.
</Gotchas>

<Instructions>
<Workflow - Team Radar
  description="Step-by-step facilitation guide for Team Radar."
>
1. [Setup] (Before session)
   - Select 6–10 dimensions relevant to your team context (don't use generic ones without customization)
   - For each dimension, write a clear 1-sentence definition and anchor descriptions for 1, 5, and 10
   - Prepare scoring mechanism (anonymous digital survey or paper forms)
   - Have previous radar chart ready for comparison (if this isn't the first assessment)
   - Prepare a blank radar chart template for visualization

2. [Open] (5 minutes)
   - State purpose: "We're mapping our team's shape, strengths and growth areas, across the dimensions that matter for our context"
   - Walk through the selected dimensions and their definitions
   - Explain scale: 1 = struggling significantly, 5 = adequate, 10 = exceptional
   - Remind: "Rate the TEAM, not individuals. And be honest; a 6 is more useful than a polite 8."

3. [Stage: Individual Scoring] (10 minutes)
   - Purpose: Get each person's uninfluenced assessment across all dimensions
   - Prompt: "For each dimension, rate our team from 1–10. Use the anchor descriptions to calibrate. Be honest - this data is for US."
   - Facilitation tip: Score simultaneously to prevent anchoring. Use anonymous forms if power dynamics exist. Provide the anchor descriptions visibly so everyone calibrates similarly. Allow people to skip a dimension if they genuinely can't assess it.

4. [Stage: Aggregate & Visualize] (10 minutes)
   - Purpose: Create the team's radar chart and identify patterns
   - Prompt: "Let's see our shape. [Reveal radar chart.] What do you notice? Where are we strong? Where are our valleys?"
   - Facilitation tip: Calculate averages and plot the radar chart live (or use a prepared tool). Also note the range (min–max) for each dimension; high variance is a signal. Show the chart and let the team react before directing discussion.

5. [Stage: Discuss High Variance] (10 minutes)
   - Purpose: Explore dimensions where team members have significantly different perceptions
   - Prompt: "On [dimension X], scores ranged from 3 to 9. What's causing such different experiences of the same team?"
   - Facilitation tip: High variance often means different roles, seniority levels, or tenures experience the team differently. This is valuable insight; it doesn't need to be resolved, just understood. It may indicate that improvements benefit some members more than others.

6. [Stage: Compare to Target/History] (10 minutes)
   - Purpose: Understand trajectory and gap to desired state
   - Prompt: "Compared to [last assessment / our target profile] - where have we improved? Where have we declined? Where's the biggest gap between where we are and where we want to be?"
   - Facilitation tip: If you have a target profile (what "10" looks like for your team), overlay it on the radar. The visual gap immediately shows priority areas. If tracking over time, celebrate dimensions that improved; this validates that effort works.

7. [Stage: Focus Selection] (10 minutes)
   - Purpose: Choose 1–2 dimensions for active improvement
   - Prompt: "We can't improve everywhere at once. Which 1–2 dimensions, if improved, would have the most impact on our effectiveness and satisfaction?"
   - Facilitation tip: Selection criteria: lowest absolute score, steepest decline, or highest impact (a dimension whose improvement would lift others). Vote if consensus doesn't emerge. Commit to specific initial actions, not just "improve X."

8. [Synthesize] (5 minutes)
   - Record scores, chart, variance notes, and focus selections
   - Confirm improvement actions and owners
   - Set next assessment date

9. [Close] (5 minutes)
   - Appreciate the team's honest self-assessment
   - Celebrate strengths ("We're genuinely strong at [X]; that's worth recognizing")
   - Confirm next assessment date and focus area check-in cadence
</Workflow - Team Radar>
</Instructions>

<Resources>
- The anchor descriptions for 1, 5, and 10 are critical for calibration. Without them, one person's "7" is another person's "5." Example: Communication 1 = "We learn important things after it's too late"; 5 = "Information flows most of the time with occasional gaps"; 10 = "The right people always have the right information at the right time."
- High variance on a dimension is often MORE actionable than a uniformly low score. It means different team members have fundamentally different experiences; investigate why.
- Consider having the team define their own "target radar"; what would the ideal team shape look like? This becomes the goal to work toward.
- For mature teams, have each person estimate what they think the team average will be before seeing results. The gap between expectation and reality is itself insightful.
- Revisit every 2–3 months. More frequent than monthly creates survey fatigue; less frequent than quarterly loses momentum.
</Resources>
