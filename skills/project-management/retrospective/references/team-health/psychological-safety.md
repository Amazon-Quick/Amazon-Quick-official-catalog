# Psychological Safety Retrospective

<Metadata>
Name: Psychological Safety Retrospective
Category: team-health
Aliases: Safety Retro, Trust Retrospective, Vulnerability Retro
Duration: 60–90 minutes
Team Size: 4–10
Best For: Assessing and improving the team's psychological safety; the shared belief that it's safe to take interpersonal risks
Definition: A specialized retrospective format focused specifically on assessing and improving psychological safety within the team; the shared belief that the team is safe for interpersonal risk-taking.
</Metadata>

<Gotchas>
1. Use when: When team is quiet in retrospectives (silence often indicates low safety); After team conflict or leadership change; When forming new teams and setting expectations; Quarterly maintenance check on team health; When engagement surveys show low psychological safety scores; When people are leaving and citing culture in exit interviews
2. Do NOT use when: Manager is the primary source of unsafety and insists on running the session (need neutral facilitator); Active HR issues are in play (different process needed); If running the retro itself would feel unsafe (need to build safety through smaller steps first); Team of 2 (too small for group dynamics; use 1:1 instead)
3. Manager-led safety retro: The direct manager runs the session, creating the exact power dynamic that undermines honesty. Fix: Always use a neutral facilitator; an Agile coach, HR partner, peer from another team, or external facilitator.
4. One and done: Running the safety retro once and considering it solved. Psychological safety requires ongoing attention. Fix: Establish regular cadence (quarterly minimum), track trends over time.
5. Agreements without follow-through: Creating beautiful team agreements that are never referenced or enforced. Fix: Post them visibly. Reference them in team interactions. When violated, address it (gently but consistently).
6. Forced vulnerability: Pressuring people to share before they're ready. Fix: Always allow passing. Build safety incrementally; start with anonymous inputs, graduate to open discussion over time.
7. All talk, no behavior change: Good discussion but nothing actually changes in daily interactions. Fix: Focus on 1–2 specific behavioral changes. Track them. Celebrate when people model the new behaviors.
</Gotchas>

<Instructions>
<Workflow - Psychological Safety Retrospective
  description="Step-by-step facilitation guide for Psychological Safety Retrospective."
>
1. [Setup] (1 week prior)
   - Send anonymous pre-survey using Edmondson's 7 questions (1–5 scale)
   - Compile aggregate results (never show individual responses)
   - Secure a facilitator who is NOT the direct manager (neutral party essential)
   - Prepare the space: comfortable, private, no interruptions, no recording
   - Consider ground rules about confidentiality

2. [Open] (15 minutes)
   - Facilitator models vulnerability first: share a time you made a mistake or felt unsure
   - Define psychological safety: "The shared belief that you won't be punished or humiliated for speaking up with ideas, questions, concerns, or mistakes"
   - Present aggregate survey results (anonymized): "Here's where we stand as a team"
   - Set explicit norms: "What's shared here stays here. No attribution after the session. Curiosity over judgment."
   - Acknowledge the paradox: "This session itself requires safety. If anything feels uncomfortable, you can pass."

3. [Stage: What Creates Safety] (15 minutes)
   - Purpose: Identify and reinforce behaviors that already build safety on this team
   - Prompt: "Think of a time on this team when you felt safe to speak up, take a risk, or admit a mistake. What made that possible? What did others do that helped?"
   - Facilitation tip: Start with positive; this validates that safety DOES exist to some degree and isn't being built from zero. It also creates emotional safety for the harder conversation coming next. Capture specific behaviors, not abstract feelings.

4. [Stage: What Threatens Safety] (20 minutes)
   - Purpose: Surface behaviors and patterns that undermine safety without blaming individuals
   - Prompt: "Without naming specific people - what behaviors or patterns make it harder to speak up, ask for help, or admit uncertainty on this team?"
   - Facilitation tip: Frame as patterns, not people. "Interrupting when someone is speaking" not "John interrupts me." If the conversation gets personal, redirect: "Let's keep this about behaviors we all can influence." Use anonymous sticky notes if verbal sharing feels too exposed.

5. [Stage: Personal Needs] (15 minutes)
   - Purpose: Let each person articulate what safety looks like for them specifically
   - Prompt: "What do YOU personally need to feel safe contributing fully? Everyone's threshold is different - what's yours?"
   - Facilitation tip: Round-robin format works here; each person shares briefly. This is deeply personal and different for everyone. Some need response time; others need explicit invitation; some need to know mistakes won't be held against them. Capture the diversity of needs.

6. [Stage: Team Agreements] (15 minutes)
   - Purpose: Convert insights into explicit team norms that protect and build safety
   - Prompt: "Based on what we've heard - what agreements should we make as a team about how we'll treat each other? What specific behaviors will we commit to?"
   - Facilitation tip: Agreements must be behavioral and observable. "Be respectful" is too vague. "We'll wait for someone to finish speaking before responding" is observable. Aim for 3–5 agreements. Everyone must genuinely agree (not imposed). Write them down publicly.

7. [Synthesize] (10 minutes)
   - Read back the team agreements
   - Confirm how/where they'll be posted and referenced
   - Set a check-in cadence: "When will we revisit these?"
   - Note any individual follow-ups needed (facilitator handles privately)

8. [Close] (10 minutes)
   - Thank the team for vulnerability; acknowledge this was hard
   - Facilitator shares an appreciation: "What I noticed today was..."
   - Quick round: "One word for how you're feeling right now"
   - Confirm next check-in date
   - Remind of confidentiality norm
</Workflow - Psychological Safety Retrospective>
</Instructions>

<Resources>
- YOU must model vulnerability first. If the facilitator is guarded, the team will be too. Share a genuine moment of uncertainty or mistake before asking others to do the same.
- The anonymous pre-survey is essential; it provides data that can't be contested. If aggregate scores are low, the team can't pretend everything is fine. If scores are high, the session can focus on maintenance rather than intervention.
- If trust is very low, start with fully anonymous input (digital sticky notes, anonymous surveys) and gradually build toward open discussion over multiple sessions. Don't force the team from 0 to 100 in one session.
- Pay attention to who speaks and who doesn't. Silence is data. If certain people never contribute, check in privately afterward.
- This retro can surface issues that need 1:1 follow-up rather than group discussion. Have a plan for handling sensitive disclosures. The facilitator should be prepared to connect people with resources (HR, coaching, etc.) if needed.
- The biggest signal that safety is low: the team tells you everything is fine while body language and engagement say otherwise. Look for the gap between words and behavior.
</Resources>
