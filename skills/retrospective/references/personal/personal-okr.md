# Personal OKR Review

<Metadata>
Name: Personal OKR Review
Category: personal
Aliases: Individual OKR Retro, Personal Goal Review, Personal OKR Retrospective
Duration: 30–45 minutes
Team Size: 1 (individual practice)
Best For: End-of-cycle reflection on personal goal achievement AND improvement of your personal goal-setting system
Definition: Application of OKR retrospective principles to personal and individual goals.
</Metadata>

<Gotchas>
1. Use when: End of each personal OKR cycle (typically quarterly); When personal goals are consistently unmet and you need to understand why; Before setting next quarter's personal objectives; When your personal productivity system feels broken or misaligned; After a quarter where everything went off-track to extract learning
2. Do NOT use when: You don't use OKRs or a similar structured goal framework for personal development; Mid-cycle (use weekly review check-ins instead); When you haven't completed a full cycle (need at least one full quarter of data); When life circumstances made the quarter anomalous (major crisis, health issue; show yourself grace)
3. Too many personal OKRs: Setting 5+ personal objectives and spreading yourself thin. Fix: Maximum 2–3 personal objectives, with 2–3 KRs each. If you have professional OKRs through work, personal ones should be proportionally fewer.
4. Goals for 'should' reasons: Setting OKRs because you feel you "should" (learn a language, exercise more) rather than because they're genuinely priorities. Fix: Ask "Would I choose to spend time on this over my current weekend activities?" If not, it's aspirational, not prioritized. Be honest.
5. Unmeasurable KRs: Personal KRs that can't be scored objectively. "Be healthier" isn't measurable. Fix: "Exercise 3x/week for 12 weeks" or "Reduce resting heart rate from 72 to 66 bpm"; concrete, measurable, time-bound.
6. Set and forget: Writing personal OKRs in January and not looking at them until March. Fix: Include OKR check-in in your weekly review. If they're not in your regular workflow, they're decorative.
7. Score shame: Low scores creating guilt rather than learning. Fix: Reframe: 0.3 on an ambitious goal is data about priority and capacity, not about your worth. A 0.0 tells you the goal wasn't actually a priority; remove it honestly rather than carrying guilt.
</Gotchas>

<Instructions>
<Workflow - Personal OKR Review
  description="Step-by-step facilitation guide for Personal OKR Review."
>
1. [Setup] (Before session)
   - Pull up your personal OKRs for the ending cycle
   - Gather measurement data for each Key Result
   - Review your weekly reviews/journal for context on how the quarter felt
   - Have your next quarter dates visible for forward planning

2. [Open] (3 minutes)
   - Ground yourself: "I'm here to learn from my goals; both the ones I hit and the ones I missed. This isn't judgment; it's calibration."
   - Open your OKR document
   - Note your emotional state about the quarter before looking at scores

3. [Stage: Score Key Results] (10 minutes)
   - Purpose: Establish factual baseline - how did each Key Result land?
   - Prompt: "Rate each Key Result from 0.0 to 1.0. Be honest. 0.7 is the intended sweet spot for well-set OKRs."
   - Facilitation tip: Score dispassionately first. 0.0 = no progress. 0.3 = some progress. 0.5 = halfway. 0.7 = strong progress (ideal). 1.0 = fully achieved (either excellent or possibly sandbagged). Look at the pattern: are most KRs in the 0.6–0.8 range? That suggests good goal-setting. All 1.0? You aimed too low. All 0.2? Either too ambitious or not prioritized.

4. [Stage: Achievement Reflection] (10 minutes)
   - Purpose: Understand what enabled hits and what blocked misses
   - Prompt: "For my highest-scoring KRs - what helped? What habits, systems, or conditions enabled progress? For my lowest - what blocked me? Time? Priority? Unclear action? External factors?"
   - Facilitation tip: Be honest about patterns. Common blockers: set ambitious goals then didn't protect time for them, other priorities crowded them out, the KR was aspirational but not connected to daily actions, life happened. Common enablers: clear daily habits, accountability (shared with someone), connected to intrinsic motivation, visible progress tracking.

5. [Stage: Goal Quality Assessment] (5 minutes)
   - Purpose: Evaluate whether these were the right goals to set in the first place
   - Prompt: "Were these the right goals? Looking back - did these Objectives represent my true priorities? Were the Key Results measurable and meaningful? Did I set too many?"
   - Facilitation tip: Common issues: too many OKRs (diluted focus), OKRs set for "should" reasons rather than genuine priorities, KRs that weren't measurable ("improve my health" vs "exercise 3x/week"), mixing professional and personal without adequate capacity for both.

6. [Stage: System Review] (10 minutes)
   - Purpose: Assess whether your personal productivity and goal-tracking system supported OKR execution
   - Prompt: "Did my personal system support these goals? Did I check in regularly? Were OKRs visible in my daily workflow? Did I make decisions with OKRs in mind, or did they sit forgotten in a document?"
   - Facilitation tip: Key diagnostic questions: How often did you look at your OKRs during the quarter? (If < weekly, they weren't operational). Did your weekly review reference OKRs? Did you ever say "no" to something because of your OKRs? If OKRs weren't influencing daily decisions, the system needs redesign - not just better goals.

7. [Stage: Adjustments for Next Cycle] (5 minutes)
   - Purpose: Define what changes to make in your personal OKR process
   - Prompt: "What will I do differently next quarter? Fewer OKRs? Better check-in cadence? More measurable KRs? Different tracking system? Share with accountability partner?"
   - Facilitation tip: Pick 1–2 process changes maximum. Common adjustments: reduce to 2 personal objectives (from 4+), make check-in part of weekly review, make KRs more measurable, include only goals you'd prioritize over other uses of your time, share with a mentor for accountability.

8. [Synthesize] (3 minutes)
   - Record scores, key learnings, and process adjustments
   - Note inputs for next quarter's OKR drafting
   - Set date for next quarter's OKR-setting session (separate from this retro)

9. [Close] (2 minutes)
   - Acknowledge: regardless of scores, having personal goals and reviewing them puts you ahead of most
   - Note: "My top adjustment for next cycle is [X]"
   - Close the document. Let insights settle before writing next quarter's goals.
</Workflow - Personal OKR Review>
</Instructions>

<Resources>
- Keep personal OKRs to 2–3 objectives maximum. You have a job, relationships, health, and interests competing for the same 168 hours per week. Personal OKRs must fit within remaining capacity after professional obligations.
- Include both professional development AND personal wellbeing goals. All professional goals leads to burnout; all personal goals ignores career growth. Balance is individual; but both dimensions deserve representation.
- The "system review" is where the most durable improvements live. Better goals without a better system will produce the same results. Focus on: visibility (can you see your OKRs daily?), cadence (do you check in weekly?), and integration (are OKRs part of decision-making?).
- Consider sharing personal OKRs with a mentor, coach, or accountability partner. The social element dramatically increases follow-through - not because of pressure, but because of commitment and support.
- Be honest about "performative goals"; OKRs you set because they look good but you have no real intention of prioritizing. It's better to have 1 genuine objective than 4 aspirational ones you'll ignore. Honesty in goal-setting is itself a skill worth developing.
- Separate the scoring session from the next-quarter goal-setting session by at least a few days. Scoring in the same session as goal-setting leads to reactive goal-setting ("I missed fitness, so I'll double down") rather than thoughtful goal-setting.
</Resources>
