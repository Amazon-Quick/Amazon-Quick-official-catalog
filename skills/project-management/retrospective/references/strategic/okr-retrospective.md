# OKR Retrospective

<Metadata>
Name: OKR Retrospective
Category: strategic
Aliases: OKR Review, End-of-Cycle Review, Goal Retrospective, OKR Reflection
Duration: 60–90 minutes
Team Size: 4–15
Best For: End-of-cycle reflection on OKR process effectiveness, not just goal scoring, but improving how goals are set, tracked, and executed
Definition: A structured end-of-cycle meeting where teams examine how their OKR process worked, not just whether goals were hit, but how the process of setting, tracking, and executing against OKRs can be improved.
</Metadata>

<Gotchas>
1. Use when: End of each OKR cycle (typically quarterly); After the first 2–3 quarters of OKR adoption (refining the system); When OKR adoption is struggling (low engagement, sandbagged goals, missed check-ins); Before setting next quarter's OKRs (learnings inform goal-setting); When teams report OKRs feel disconnected from actual work
2. Do NOT use when: Organization doesn't use OKRs or a similar goal framework; Mid-cycle (use OKR check-ins instead); Team too new to have completed a full OKR cycle; When the problem is clearly strategy (not goal-setting process). Use Strategy Retro
3. Score and move on: Only reviewing OKR scores without examining the process that produced those scores. Fix: Explicitly separate scoring (5-minute data review) from process retrospective (the bulk of the session).
4. Only reviewing misses: Focusing exclusively on KRs scored below target while ignoring what worked. Fix: Analyze 1.0 scores too. Were they genuinely ambitious, or sandbagged? What conditions enabled success?
5. Skip to next quarter: Jumping directly from scoring to setting next quarter's OKRs without reflecting on what to change about the process. Fix: Never draft new OKRs in the same session as the retro. Let insights settle, then write.
6. Too many OKRs, never learning: Setting 5+ objectives each quarter and never reducing because "everything is important." Fix: Use the retro to enforce focus. How many OKRs could the team actually work on? Constrain next cycle accordingly.
7. Performative check-ins: Check-ins happen on paper but don't influence decisions. OKRs live in a dashboard no one looks at. Fix: Ask "When did we reference OKRs in a real decision?" If the answer is never, the operating rhythm needs redesign.
</Gotchas>

<Instructions>
<Workflow - OKR Retrospective
  description="Step-by-step facilitation guide for OKR Retrospective."
>
1. [Setup] (1 week prior)
   - Have all team members score their Key Results individually (pre-work: 0.0–1.0 scale)
   - Compile OKR scorecard with all objectives and key results scored
   - Send pre-work survey: "How well did our OKR process work this quarter?" covering check-in frequency, alignment clarity, prioritization effectiveness
   - Gather data: How many check-ins happened? Were OKRs referenced in sprint planning? How often were they discussed?
   - Prepare historical comparison if available (scores vs. previous quarters)

2. [Open] (10 minutes)
   - State purpose: "We're here to improve our OKR PROCESS, not just review scores. The question isn't just 'did we hit goals' but 'how can we get better at the entire goal-setting and execution system?'"
   - Present the aggregate scorecard (not individual deep-dives, that's a separate meeting)
   - Frame the two conversations: (1) Were these the right goals? (2) Did our process support achieving them?

3. [Stage: Score Review] (15 minutes)
   - Purpose: Establish factual baseline of OKR performance this cycle
   - Prompt: "Looking at our scores, where did we land? Any surprise scores? Any KRs that scored 1.0 (potentially sandbagged) or 0.0 (potentially too ambitious or abandoned)?"
   - Facilitation tip: Keep this brief. Scores should be pre-calculated. Focus on patterns: Are most KRs in the 0.6–0.8 sweet spot? Too many 1.0s suggest sandbagging. Too many 0.0s suggest poor prioritization or overly ambitious goals. Flag anomalies for discussion.

4. [Stage: Goal Quality Reflection] (20 minutes)
   - Purpose: Assess whether the right goals were set in the first place
   - Prompt: "Were these the right objectives? Were our Key Results truly measurable? Looking back, what would you change about how we WROTE our OKRs?"
   - Facilitation tip: Common issues: KRs that aren't measurable ("improve customer experience"), objectives that were too vague, missing objectives that should have been priorities, KRs that measured activity not outcome. Capture specific writing improvements for next cycle.

5. [Stage: Process Review] (20 minutes)
   - Purpose: Evaluate how well the OKR operating rhythm supported execution
   - Prompt: "How well did our check-ins work? Were we aware of each other's OKRs? Did OKRs help or hinder decision-making? Did we actually USE OKRs when making trade-offs?"
   - Facilitation tip: Key questions to probe: Were weekly check-ins happening? Did OKRs influence sprint planning? When faced with competing priorities, did anyone reference OKRs? If not, OKRs existed on paper but not in practice. This stage surfaces the behavioral gap.

6. [Stage: Blockers & Enablers] (15 minutes)
   - Purpose: Identify what helped or hindered OKR execution specifically
   - Prompt: "What enabled OKR progress? What blocked it? Were there structural issues: too many OKRs, unclear ownership, lack of time allocated, competing priorities?"
   - Facilitation tip: Common blockers: too many OKRs (no focus), OKRs not connected to daily work, no protected time for OKR work, mid-quarter priority changes that made OKRs irrelevant. Enablers: visible tracking, regular reviews, leadership modeling OKR behavior.

7. [Stage: Process Adjustments] (15 minutes)
   - Purpose: Define specific changes to the OKR process for next cycle
   - Prompt: "What specific changes will we make to our OKR process next quarter? Consider: number of OKRs, check-in cadence, writing approach, alignment method, and prioritization."
   - Facilitation tip: Keep adjustments small and concrete. "Better communication" is not an adjustment. "We'll review OKR progress every Monday standup for 5 minutes" is. Limit to 2–3 process changes per cycle to avoid overwhelming the system.

8. [Synthesize] (10 minutes)
   - Summarize: Score overview, goal quality findings, process health assessment, adjustments decided
   - Confirm process changes and who owns implementing them
   - Note any inputs for next quarter's OKR-setting session

9. [Close] (5 minutes)
   - Confirm timeline for next quarter's OKR drafting
   - Assign ownership for process adjustments
   - Quick poll: "On a 1–5 scale, how useful is our OKR practice? What score are we targeting next quarter?"
</Workflow - OKR Retrospective>
</Instructions>

<Resources>
- Run team-level OKR retros BEFORE company-level ones. Team insights should flow upward to inform organizational adjustments.
- Send a pre-work survey to surface process issues before the room discussion. This ensures introverts and those with sensitive feedback have a voice.
- Keep the same retrospective structure every cycle. The team learns the format and conversations deepen over time. Don't reinvent the OKR retro each quarter.
- The "OKR influence on decisions" question is the litmus test. If OKRs never influenced a trade-off decision, they're decorative. This single question reveals whether OKRs are operational or ceremonial.
- For organizations early in OKR adoption (first 2–3 quarters), weight the retro heavily toward process improvement. Scores will be unreliable early on. The goal is improving the system, not optimizing scores.
- Consider splitting into two sessions: 30 minutes for scoring review, 60 minutes for process retro. Mixing them often results in spending all time on individual KR scores and no time on systemic improvement.
</Resources>
