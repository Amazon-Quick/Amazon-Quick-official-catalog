# Project Post-Mortem

<Metadata>
Name: Project Post-Mortem
Category: project
Aliases: Project Debrief, End-of-Project Review, Project Autopsy
Duration: 2–4 hours
Team Size: 8–25
Best For: Thorough examination of a completed project's lifecycle, planning through delivery, to extract organizational learning
Definition: A thorough review conducted after a project completes (successfully or unsuccessfully) examining the entire project lifecycle: planning, execution, delivery, and outcomes.
</Metadata>

<Gotchas>
1. Use when: After any significant project (successful or failed). Learn from both; When organizational learning is a strategic priority; After projects that ran significantly over budget or schedule; After innovative or novel projects with new lessons to capture; When multiple teams collaborated and need shared understanding
2. Do NOT use when: Small, routine projects where overhead isn't justified; When the team has fully disbanded and moved on (too late for meaningful dialogue); During active project execution (too early. Wait for completion); When there's no appetite or mechanism for implementing changes
3. Failures only: Only conducting post-mortems for failed projects. Successful projects have equally valuable lessons. Fix: Make post-mortems standard for all significant projects regardless of outcome.
4. Report in a drawer: Post-mortem report that no one ever reads. Fix: Present findings actively to future project teams at kickoff. Integrate into templates and checklists.
5. Status meeting in disguise: Turning the post-mortem into a project status presentation rather than a reflective discussion. Fix: Data is input to discussion, not the discussion itself.
6. Too late to matter: Running the post-mortem months after completion when details are hazy. Fix: Schedule within 1–2 weeks of project close.
7. Missing voices: Only inviting the delivery team without sponsors, customers, or adjacent teams. Fix: Include anyone with material perspective on the project.
</Gotchas>

<Instructions>
<Workflow - Project Post-Mortem
  description="Step-by-step facilitation guide for Project Post-Mortem."
>
1. [Setup] (1–2 weeks prior)
   - Schedule within 1–2 weeks of project completion (before memories fade)
   - Gather quantitative data: budget variance, schedule variance, quality metrics, customer satisfaction, team velocity trends
   - Send pre-work survey to all participants: key successes, key challenges, what they'd do differently
   - Prepare project timeline visualization showing planned vs. actual milestones
   - Identify participants: full delivery team + key stakeholders + project sponsor
   - Secure a neutral facilitator (not the project manager)

2. [Open] (15 minutes)
   - Read the Retrospective Prime Directive
   - State purpose: "We're conducting a thorough examination of this project to learn, both from our successes and our struggles"
   - Present the project overview: original goals, final outcomes, team composition, key metrics
   - Set ground rules: blameless inquiry, evidence-based, constructive, confidential where needed

3. [Stage: Timeline Walk] (30 minutes)
   - Purpose: Establish shared factual understanding of what happened chronologically
   - Prompt: "Let's walk through the project timeline together. At each phase, what were the key events, decisions, and turning points?"
   - Facilitation tip: Use a visual timeline on the wall. Walk through chronologically from kickoff to close. Mark decision points, surprises, and pivots. This creates shared factual ground before interpretation begins.

4. [Stage: Plan vs. Reality] (25 minutes)
   - Purpose: Understand where and why execution diverged from the plan
   - Prompt: "Looking at what was planned versus what happened, where were the biggest gaps? What caused the divergence?"
   - Facilitation tip: Use data here: show planned timelines, budgets, and scope against actuals. Avoid blame by asking "What forces caused this divergence?" rather than "Who caused this?"

5. [Stage: Successes Deep-Dive] (25 minutes)
   - Purpose: Understand what drove positive outcomes so it can be repeated
   - Prompt: "What went right? Not just what happened, but WHY did it go right? What conditions, decisions, or practices enabled success?"
   - Facilitation tip: Teams often rush past successes to get to problems. Resist this. Understanding success drivers is as valuable as understanding failures. Push for root causes of success.

6. [Stage: Challenges Deep-Dive] (30 minutes)
   - Purpose: Identify root causes of difficulties, not just symptoms
   - Prompt: "What were our biggest challenges? For each, let's ask: Was this predictable? Was it preventable? What would have needed to be different?"
   - Facilitation tip: Use 5 Whys on the top 3 challenges. Distinguish between challenges from poor decisions vs. challenges from external factors. Both are valid learning.

7. [Stage: Process & People] (20 minutes)
   - Purpose: Evaluate the methodology, tools, and collaboration effectiveness
   - Prompt: "How effective were our processes, communication, and collaboration? What about stakeholder management?"
   - Facilitation tip: This is where interpersonal dynamics surface. Maintain blameless framing. Focus on "the system" rather than individuals. If specific relationship issues emerge, note them for private follow-up.

8. [Stage: Recommendations] (20 minutes)
   - Purpose: Convert insights into specific, actionable guidance for future projects
   - Prompt: "Based on everything we've discussed, what are our top 5 recommendations for the next team that undertakes something similar?"
   - Facilitation tip: Force prioritization. A post-mortem that produces 30 recommendations is useless. Push for the vital few. Each recommendation must be specific and actionable.

9. [Synthesize] (15 minutes)
   - Facilitator summarizes key findings across all stages
   - Review and confirm the top recommendations
   - Assign ownership for the post-mortem report
   - Identify distribution list and presentation schedule

10. [Close] (10 minutes)
    - Acknowledge team contributions to the project and to this session
    - Confirm post-mortem report timeline and next steps
    - Round: "One thing you're taking forward from this project personally"
    - Express gratitude for candor and vulnerability
</Workflow - Project Post-Mortem>
</Instructions>

<Resources>
- Use a neutral facilitator. Never the project manager who may be defensive about decisions made. An external coach or PM from another team works well.
- Send the quantitative data package in advance. Don't waste session time presenting numbers. People should arrive having reviewed the data.
- The chronological timeline walk is essential for large projects. It establishes shared factual ground and often reveals that different people had completely different understandings of what happened.
- For projects that failed or had significant interpersonal challenges, consider splitting into two sessions: one for process/technical learning, one for relationship/collaboration learning.
- Always celebrate what went right before diving into problems. Post-mortems have a negativity bias. Counteract it deliberately.
- Keep a "parking lot" for items that need 1:1 follow-up rather than group discussion (especially interpersonal issues).
</Resources>
