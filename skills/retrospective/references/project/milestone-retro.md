# Milestone Retrospective

<Metadata>
Name: Milestone Retrospective
Category: project
Aliases: Release Retrospective, Delivery Retrospective, Checkpoint Retro
Duration: 60–90 minutes
Team Size: 5–20
Best For: Mid-project reflection at significant delivery points, enabling course correction while the project is still active
Definition: A mid-project retrospective conducted at significant milestones (releases, deliveries, quarterly checkpoints) rather than waiting for project completion.
</Metadata>

<Gotchas>
1. Use when: After major releases or product deliveries; At quarterly checkpoints on long-running projects; When a project spans multiple months or quarters; When sprint-level retros aren't capturing bigger patterns; After completing a significant body of work that represents a meaningful delivery
2. Do NOT use when: Short projects (less than 1 month) where sprint retros suffice; When sprint retrospectives are already capturing all relevant insights; When no clear milestones are defined (define them first); At project end (use post-mortem or lessons learned instead)
3. Status meeting disguise: Turning the milestone retro into a status report rather than a reflective discussion. Fix: Present data as input (5 minutes), then facilitate reflection and discussion (the bulk of the session).
4. Sprint retro repeat: Discussing the same items that were covered in individual sprint retros without looking for bigger patterns. Fix: Explicitly ask "What patterns span multiple sprints?" and "What did sprint retros surface but not solve?"
5. No course correction: Identifying issues but not making changes because "we're mid-project and can't change now." Fix: The entire point is course correction. If you can't change, why reflect? Even small adjustments compound.
6. Waiting for project end: Skipping milestone retros because "we'll do a full post-mortem later." Fix: By project end, it's too late to benefit the current project. Milestone retros create value NOW.
7. Core team only: Not inviting stakeholders who received the milestone delivery. Fix: Their perspective on what was delivered vs. expected is invaluable data for course correction.
</Gotchas>

<Instructions>
<Workflow - Milestone Retrospective
  description="Step-by-step facilitation guide for Milestone Retrospective."
>
1. [Setup] (2–3 days prior)
   - Define the milestone scope: what was delivered, what timeframe is being reflected on
   - Gather data: velocity trends, quality metrics, customer feedback on the delivery, budget burn rate
   - Send pre-work: "Reflect on the work from [start date] to [milestone date]. What patterns do you notice?"
   - Invite broader audience than sprint retros: include stakeholders affected by the milestone
   - Prepare a visual showing sprint-level data aggregated across the milestone period

2. [Open] (10 minutes)
   - Celebrate: "We hit milestone X. Let's acknowledge what was delivered before we reflect."
   - State scope: "We're looking at the period from [date] to [date], bigger patterns than a single sprint"
   - Present key data: delivery metrics, quality indicators, stakeholder feedback
   - Distinguish from sprint retro: "This is about patterns and trends, not individual sprint events"

3. [Stage: Milestone Achievement] (15 minutes)
   - Purpose: Assess how close to plan the milestone delivery was
   - Prompt: "Did we hit the milestone as planned? What was delivered vs. what was intended? Where are the gaps?"
   - Facilitation tip: Use data: show planned scope vs. actual scope. Don't just say "we delivered." Quantify completeness, quality, and timing. Acknowledge partial delivery honestly.

4. [Stage: What Worked] (15 minutes)
   - Purpose: Identify practices and patterns that contributed to success over the milestone period
   - Prompt: "Looking across the past [N] weeks/sprints, what practices, approaches, or decisions consistently helped us?"
   - Facilitation tip: Look for patterns that span multiple sprints. Things that were "fine" in any single sprint but compound over time (positive or negative). Silent writing (5 min) then share and cluster.

5. [Stage: What Struggled] (15 minutes)
   - Purpose: Surface challenges that persisted or grew across the milestone period
   - Prompt: "What challenges kept appearing sprint after sprint? What got worse over time? What almost derailed us?"
   - Facilitation tip: Focus on persistent issues, things that sprint retros identified but didn't resolve. These are the systemic issues that need milestone-level attention. Use 5 Whys on the top 2–3.

6. [Stage: Team Dynamics] (10 minutes)
   - Purpose: Check on collaboration health across the longer timeframe
   - Prompt: "How well are we collaborating? Are there communication patterns, dependency issues, or team health concerns that have built up?"
   - Facilitation tip: This is where burnout, collaboration friction, and role confusion surface. These issues often aren't visible in individual sprints but compound over milestone periods.

7. [Stage: Course Corrections] (15 minutes)
   - Purpose: Define specific changes for the next milestone period (this is the key advantage, we CAN still change)
   - Prompt: "Given what we've learned, what specific changes will we make for the next milestone period? What should we start, stop, or adjust?"
   - Facilitation tip: The power of milestone retros is course correction. Unlike project post-mortems, we're still in the project. Frame actions as changes that will affect the next phase. Limit to 3–5 concrete changes.

8. [Synthesize] (10 minutes)
   - Summarize: milestone assessment, top patterns (positive and negative), course corrections decided
   - Update the project plan based on learnings
   - Assign owners to course correction actions
   - Communicate key stakeholder-relevant findings

9. [Close] (5 minutes)
   - Confirm action owners and timelines
   - Set next milestone retro date
   - Thank participants, especially stakeholders who joined beyond the core team
   - Quick round: "One word for how you're feeling about the next phase"
</Workflow - Milestone Retrospective>
</Instructions>

<Resources>
- The unique value of milestone retros is their timing: they're the only retrospective format that examines multi-sprint patterns while there's still time to act. Emphasize this in framing.
- Bring aggregated data from the sprint period, not just the latest sprint's metrics, but trends over 4–8 sprints. Velocity trends, defect trends, cycle time trends reveal patterns invisible at sprint level.
- Include stakeholders who received the milestone delivery. Their feedback on what was delivered vs. what they expected is critical input that the delivery team doesn't have.
- If the project has multiple teams, consider a cross-team milestone retro in addition to team-specific ones. This is where dependency issues and integration challenges surface.
- Keep a "persistent issues" tracker across milestone retros. If the same issue appears in two consecutive milestone retros, it needs escalation or a fundamentally different approach. The current approach isn't working.
</Resources>
