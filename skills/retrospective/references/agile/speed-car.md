# Speed Car (Race Car)

<Metadata>
Name: Speed Car (Race Car)
Category: agile
Aliases: Race Car Retro, Speed Car, Engine & Parachute, Formula 1 Retro
Duration: 30-40 minutes
Team Size: 3-8 (max 10)
Best For: Quick, energetic retrospective for teams that respond to competition and speed metaphors
Definition: A racing metaphor retrospective where the team's performance is represented as a race car with an Engine (forces driving forward) and a Parachute (forces creating drag), optionally adding Pit Crew (supporters) and Abyss (risks), designed to be fast, energetic, and action-oriented within a tight timebox.
</Metadata>

<Gotchas>
1. Use when: Need a quick, energetic retrospective; team is bored with standard formats and needs a fresh metaphor; velocity/speed is the primary concern; limited meeting time (30-40 minutes max); competitive teams that respond to racing language
2. Do NOT use when: "Speed" narrative is harmful (burnout risk, quality suffering); emotional or interpersonal issues need addressing; careful, slow deliberation is needed; team is already moving too fast and cutting corners
3. Speed glorification: Using this format when the team is burned out or cutting corners reinforces harmful "go faster" pressure. Fix: Only use when speed optimization is genuinely appropriate, not when the team needs rest.
4. Long discussions: This format should be FAST. If you're spending 60 minutes, you've lost the energy. Fix: Strictly timebox everything to 40 minutes total. No extensions.
5. Ignoring the Pit Crew: The recognition element matters. Fix: Don't skip gratitude. Always include a shoutout for someone who supported the team.
6. Too many parachutes, no engines: The team only sees drag. Fix: Insist on balanced contribution. Start with engines to build energy.
7. Over-complicating the visual: Adding too many elements (tires, fuel, track conditions) dilutes the simplicity. Fix: Stick to Engine + Parachute core, with optional Pit Crew and Abyss only.
</Gotchas>

<Instructions>
<Workflow - Speed Car
  description="Facilitation guide for Speed Car (Race Car) retrospective."
>
1. [Setup] Draw a race car on the board with: an engine (front), a parachute (behind, creating drag). Optionally add: pit crew (side), and an abyss/cliff (ahead). Use colored sticky notes. Allocate time: 5 min brainstorm, 5 min placement, 10 min voting/discussion, 10 min actions, 3 min close. Total 40 minutes STRICT.
2. [Open] (3 minutes)
   - Read the Prime Directive
   - Frame: "We're a race car. Some things are our ENGINE, making us fast. Some things are our PARACHUTE, creating drag. Let's figure out how to go faster."
   - Energy: Keep the tone upbeat and fast-paced. Match the metaphor.
3. [Stage: Engine & Parachute - Looking Back] (5 minutes)
   - Purpose: Identify driving and dragging forces from the recent sprint
   - Prompt: "Looking at last sprint: What was our ENGINE? What made us go fast, deliver well, feel momentum? And what was our PARACHUTE? What slowed us down, created drag, held us back?"
   - Facilitation tip: Set a timer. 5 minutes. Fast. The format itself should feel like a race.
4. [Stage: Pit Crew & Abyss - Looking Forward (Optional)] (5 minutes)
   - Purpose: Recognize support and identify upcoming risks
   - Prompt: "Who or what is our PIT CREW? Who supports us, fixes things, keeps us running? And what ABYSS or cliff is ahead, what risk could take us off the track entirely?"
   - Facilitation tip: Pit Crew is a recognition element. Call out specific people or teams by name.
5. [Stage: Place on Visual] (3 minutes)
   - Purpose: Create the full visual picture
   - Prompt: "Stick your items on the car. Engine up front, parachute behind, pit crew to the side, abyss ahead."
   - Facilitation tip: Quick placement, no lengthy explanations. The visual tells the story.
6. [Stage: Dot-Vote] (3 minutes)
   - Purpose: Democratically prioritize what to act on
   - Prompt: "3 dots each. Vote on the items with the biggest impact, either the most powerful engine to amplify or the heaviest parachute to cut."
   - Facilitation tip: Voting across both categories. Don't restrict to just parachutes (problems).
7. [Stage: Quick Discussion] (10 minutes)
   - Purpose: Convert top-voted items into fast, specific actions
   - Prompt: "Top Engine: How do we get MORE of this? Top Parachute: How do we CUT it? Give me a one-sentence action for each."
   - Facilitation tip: Fast discussion. If it takes more than 3 minutes per item, table it and move on.
8. [Synthesize] 2-3 actions max. Keep them tight and specific. This is a sprint, not a marathon.
9. [Close] (3 minutes)
   - Read actions (should be 2-3, no more)
   - Finish with energy: "Engines revved. Parachutes cut. Let's race."
   - Quick pit crew shoutout: Acknowledge one person/team who supported the sprint.
</Workflow - Speed Car>
</Instructions>

<Resources>
- Match the ENERGY of the metaphor. This should feel fast, crisp, dynamic. Not a slow reflective session.
- Strictly timebox everything. 40 minutes total. No extensions. This format respects speed in its own execution.
- The Pit Crew element adds humanity to what could otherwise be a purely mechanical format. Include it.
- For competitive teams, you can gamify: "We had 5 parachutes last sprint, 3 this sprint. We're accelerating!"
- Works particularly well with engineering teams who think in systems (engines, drag, optimization)
- Don't use this every sprint. It's a high-energy format best rotated in occasionally for variety
- For remote teams, use a race car template in Miro/FigJam. The visual engagement matters.
</Resources>
