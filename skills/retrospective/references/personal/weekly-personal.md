# Weekly Personal Retrospective

<Metadata>
Name: Weekly Personal Retrospective
Category: personal
Aliases: Weekly Review, GTD Review, Personal Sprint Retro, Weekly Reflection
Duration: 30–60 minutes
Team Size: 1 (individual practice)
Best For: Regular personal ritual for closing open loops, reflecting on the week, and setting intentional priorities for the next week
Definition: A regular personal ritual (typically weekly) where an individual reflects on the past week, closes open loops, and plans the upcoming week.
</Metadata>

<Gotchas>
1. Use when: Weekly, on a consistent day and time (habit formation is critical); When feeling overwhelmed or unfocused and needing to regain clarity; When professional growth is a priority requiring intentional tracking; As input to performance reviews (creates a running record); When too many commitments are slipping through the cracks
2. Do NOT use when: When life is in genuine crisis (need support, not structure); If the practice becomes a source of guilt rather than clarity; When the review itself takes longer than the work it's reviewing (simplify)
3. Too long: The review takes 2+ hours and becomes a dreaded obligation. Fix: Strict 60-minute maximum. If your systems need more than an hour to review, simplify your systems.
4. Pure productivity, no reflection: Only clearing inboxes and updating lists without reflecting on learning, growth, or wellbeing. Fix: Include the "Reflect" stage explicitly. Productivity without meaning leads to burnout.
5. Guilt generator: The review becomes a weekly exercise in shame about uncompleted tasks. Fix: Reframe: items not done are data about priorities, not moral failures. Ask "Should this still be on my list?" rather than "Why didn't I do this?"
6. Skipping the hard weeks: Only doing the review when things are going well; skipping when overwhelmed (precisely when it's most needed). Fix: On hard weeks, do a 15-minute mini-review. Something is always better than nothing.
7. No consistent cadence: Doing the review sporadically rather than on a fixed schedule. Fix: Same day, same time, every week. Block it in your calendar. Treat it as a non-negotiable appointment with yourself.
</Gotchas>

<Instructions>
<Workflow - Weekly Personal Retrospective
  description="Step-by-step facilitation guide for Weekly Personal Retrospective."
>
1. [Setup] (Recurring calendar block)
   - Block 30–60 minutes on the same day/time each week (Friday afternoon or Sunday evening work well)
   - Protect this time; no meetings, no interruptions
   - Have your tools ready: task manager, calendar, notes, journal
   - Prepare your environment: quiet space, favorite beverage, minimal distractions

2. [Open] (2 minutes)
   - Take 3 deep breaths. Transition from "doing" mode to "reflecting" mode.
   - Set intention: "I'm here to get clear, get current, and get creative about the week ahead"
   - Open your review template/journal

3. [Stage: Get Clear; Empty Inboxes] (10 minutes)
   - Purpose: Capture all open loops so nothing lives only in your head
   - Prompt: "What's on my mind that isn't captured anywhere? What inboxes need processing?"
   - Facilitation tip: Process all inboxes to zero (or at least triaged): email inbox, physical inbox, notes app, voicemails, browser tabs, mental worries. For each item: delete, delegate, defer (add to task list), do (if < 2 min), or file. The goal is zero items in "limbo."

4. [Stage: Get Current; Review Systems] (15 minutes)
   - Purpose: Ensure all project lists, next actions, and calendar are accurate and current
   - Prompt: "Are my project lists up to date? Does every active project have a next action? Does my calendar reflect reality for the next 2 weeks?"
   - Facilitation tip: Review each active project, is it still active? Does it have a defined next action? Review your calendar for the past week (anything to follow up on?) and upcoming 2 weeks (anything to prepare for?). Review your "waiting for" list, anything overdue? Review your "someday/maybe" list; anything ready to activate?

5. [Stage: Reflect; Week in Review] (10 minutes)
   - Purpose: Extract learning and acknowledgment from the past week
   - Prompt: "What did I accomplish this week? What didn't get done - and why? What did I learn? What am I grateful for?"
   - Facilitation tip: Don't skip this stage. Pure productivity reviews without reflection create hamster-wheel burnout. Celebrate wins (even small ones). Acknowledge challenges without judgment. Notice patterns: are the same things consistently not getting done? That's data about priorities or capacity.

6. [Stage: Get Creative; Next Week Intentions] (10 minutes)
   - Purpose: Set intentional priorities for the upcoming week aligned with longer-term goals
   - Prompt: "What are my top 3 priorities for next week? What would make next week a success? Is there anything on my someday/maybe list ready to activate?"
   - Facilitation tip: Limit to 3 priorities (not 10). These should be the things that, if accomplished, would make the week a success regardless of what else happens. Review quarterly/annual goals; are weekly priorities feeding them? If not, something is misaligned.

7. [Synthesize] (3 minutes)
   - Record your top 3 priorities somewhere visible (sticky note, phone wallpaper, planner)
   - Note any key insights or patterns from the reflection
   - Close all review tools and transition out

8. [Close] (2 minutes)
   - Take a breath. You're current. You're clear. You've reflected.
   - Note one thing you're looking forward to next week
   - Close your workspace. The review is complete.
</Workflow - Weekly Personal Retrospective>
</Instructions>

<Resources>
- Friday afternoon works for many people (close the work week cleanly), but Sunday evening works for others (prepare for Monday with clarity). Experiment and pick what sticks.
- Consistency > perfection. A 20-minute review done every week is infinitely more valuable than a 90-minute review done sporadically. Start small and expand only if you want to.
- Include a wellbeing dimension - not just "What did I produce?" but "How am I?" Sleep, exercise, mood, relationships. Sustainable productivity requires sustainable health.
- Keep a running "wins" log as you go through the week (jot a note when something goes well). This makes the weekly reflection easier and prevents recency bias.
- The "Get Creative" stage is where you connect weekly tactics to longer-term goals. If your top 3 priorities never relate to your quarterly goals, you have an alignment problem worth noticing.
- Consider maintaining a "weekly review streak"; how many consecutive weeks have you completed the review? The streak itself becomes motivating.
</Resources>
