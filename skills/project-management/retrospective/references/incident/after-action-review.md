# After Action Review (AAR)

<Metadata>
Name: After Action Review (AAR)
Category: incident
Aliases: AAR, Debrief, Military Debrief, Hot Wash, Post-Event Debrief
Duration: 15 minutes to 3 hours (scales with event complexity)
Team Size: 4–15; max 30+
Best For: Any significant event (success OR failure) where execution deviated from the plan. The most portable and universal incident retrospective format
Definition: A structured debrief format developed by the U.S. Army that examines four questions (what was supposed to happen, what actually happened, why there was a difference, and what to do next time) designed to learn from both successes and failures without assigning blame, refined over 50 years of military operations and adopted across business, healthcare, emergency services, and government.
</Metadata>

<Gotchas>
1. Use when: after any significant event (successes AND failures); when execution deviated from plan positively or negatively; immediately after events while memory is fresh (same-day/next-day); when you need a portable universal format for any team size; after launches, releases, presentations, sales calls, training; when building a debrief habit; for events needing structured but lightweight format
2. Do NOT use when: ongoing continuous work without discrete events or a clear plan to compare against; when "the plan" didn't exist or was never articulated; routine operations without notable learning moments; when deep technical root-cause analysis is needed (use Five Whys or Timeline instead); when the event is too complex for a single meeting and requires multi-session investigation
3. Senior leader speaks first. This is the #1 AAR killer; everyone else conforms to the boss's view. Fix: leaders speak last or submit written observations first; brief senior leaders beforehand that their job is to listen
4. Only conducting AARs after failures. The Army does AARs after EVERY exercise including successes; "Why did this go well?" is equally valuable. Fix: mandate AARs after all significant events regardless of outcome
5. Spending all time on Questions 1–3 and rushing Question 4. This produces a history lesson, not a learning event. Fix: allocate 50% of time to improvements (Question 4); flip the ratio
6. Vague improvements. "Communicate better" is not actionable. Fix: require specific commitments with owners and next steps (e.g., "establish a dedicated incident channel within 2 minutes of detection")
7. AAR document that never gets distributed. It teaches only the people in the room. Fix: publish findings within 24 hours, update SOPs, share with adjacent teams
8. Facilitator who is also the leader. The dual role suppresses candor. Fix: always use a separate facilitator, even a peer from another team
9. Treating AAR as performance evaluation. The moment people fear findings will appear in their review, candor dies. Fix: separate learning from evaluation absolutely
</Gotchas>

<Instructions>
<Workflow - After Action Review (AAR)
  description="Facilitation guide for After Action Review."
>
1. [Setup] (5–10 minutes before)
   - Identify facilitator, who should NOT be the leader or primary decision-maker (reduces influence bias)
   - Secure a space where all participants can gather (or virtual equivalent)
   - Write the four AAR questions visibly (whiteboard, slide, shared doc)
   - Review the original plan/intent/objectives so you can reference them
   - Conduct within 24–48 hours of the event; sooner is better (memory degrades rapidly)

2. [Open] (3–5 minutes)
   - Purpose: Establish psychological safety and "no rank in the room" norm
   - Prompt: "This is an After Action Review. There's no rank in this room; everyone's perspective is equally valid. We're here to learn, not to blame. The most junior person's observation may be the most important insight. I'll facilitate; the leader is a participant like everyone else."
   - Facilitation tip: In the Army, officers explicitly remove rank insignia for AARs. In business, the equivalent is having the most senior person speak LAST, not first. If the VP speaks first, everyone else's answers will conform. Brief senior leaders beforehand.

3. [Stage: What Was Supposed to Happen?] (10–15% of time)
   - Purpose: Establish the baseline - the plan, intent, objectives, and success criteria
   - Prompt: "What was our plan? What were we trying to accomplish? What did success look like? What standards were we operating against?"
   - Facilitation tip: This sounds simple but often reveals misalignment. If different people articulate different plans or different success criteria, you've already found a learning. Don't skip this question even when the plan seems obvious; articulating it surfaces assumptions.

4. [Stage: What Actually Happened?] (15–20% of time)
   - Purpose: Establish facts - what occurred, in what sequence, what was the outcome
   - Prompt: "What actually happened? Walk us through the key events. What did we observe? What were the actual results versus our plan?"
   - Facilitation tip: Keep this factual. Use "I saw..." and "I observed..." language. If memories conflict, capture both and note the discrepancy. Resist the urge to analyze here; that's Question 3. Build the shared picture first.

5. [Stage: Why Was There a Difference?] (25–30% of time)
   - Purpose: Analyze the gap between plan and reality - root causes of both shortfalls AND successes
   - Prompt: "Why did reality differ from the plan? What caused the gap? What conditions, decisions, or factors led to the outcome - both the things that went wrong AND the things that went better than expected?"
   - Facilitation tip: This is where blameless framing is tested. When someone says "because Bob didn't..." redirect: "What conditions made the right action difficult or non-obvious?" Apply to positives too: "Why did the failover work so well?" has as much learning value as "Why did detection take so long?"

6. [Stage: What Will We Do Differently Next Time?] (50% of time)
   - Purpose: Define specific sustainments (what to keep) and improvements (what to change)
   - Prompt: "What will we sustain - continue doing because it worked? What will we improve - change because it didn't? Be specific: who will do what differently, by when?"
   - Facilitation tip: The Army allocates 50% of AAR time here; it's the most important question. Split into SUSTAIN (things that worked; don't lose these) and IMPROVE (things to change). Every improvement needs an owner and a next step. Vague intentions ("we should communicate better") are worthless; specific commitments ("we'll establish a dedicated incident channel for incident response and route all comms there") have value.

7. [Synthesize] (5 minutes)
   - Purpose: Identify systemic patterns and broader applicability
   - Prompt: "Looking at our sustainments and improvements together - are there systemic patterns? Do these lessons apply to other teams or situations? What would we tell a team about to do something similar?"
   - Facilitation tip: Pattern recognition separates good AARs from great ones. "This is the third time communication breakdown was an improvement area" means you have a systemic issue, not a one-off.

8. [Close] (3 minutes)
   - Purpose: Confirm commitments, assign documentation, express appreciation
   - Prompt: "Let's confirm our top 3 sustainments and top 3 improvements. Who will document this and share it? Who owns each improvement action? Thank you all for candid participation."
   - Facilitation tip: Document and distribute within 24 hours. Update SOPs, training materials, or playbooks based on findings. An AAR that lives only in people's heads teaches only those people; written AARs teach the organization.
</Workflow - After Action Review (AAR)>
</Instructions>

<Resources>
- **The AAR scales elegantly.** 15 minutes for a sales call debrief. 45 minutes for a product launch. 3 hours for a multi-day crisis response. Same four questions; different depth. Don't overcomplicate it.
- **"No rank in the room" is the hardest norm to establish and the most important.** Brief leaders privately before the AAR: "Your job is to listen, affirm the learning culture, and speak last. Your participation is as an equal observer, not as the evaluator."
- **Conduct AARs as close to the event as possible.** Ideally within hours. Memory degrades by 50% within 24 hours. The "hot wash" (immediate brief debrief) captures what's lost by the next day.
- **For complex events, structure Question 2 chronologically.** Have different people narrate their phases/perspectives. This naturally reveals information gaps and coordination issues.
- **Track AAR improvements over time.** If the same improvement appears in 3 consecutive AARs and nothing has changed, escalate. The AAR process is working (identifying issues) but the improvement process is failing (not implementing changes).
- **The AAR is a habit, not an event.** The most effective teams do AARs after every significant activity - weekly, sometimes daily. Frequency builds the muscle of reflection and continuous improvement.
- **For remote/hybrid teams**, use a shared document projected for all. Have people write observations first (2 minutes silent), then discuss. This prevents anchoring and ensures all voices contribute.
</Resources>
