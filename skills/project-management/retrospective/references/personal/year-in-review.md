# Year-in-Review

<Metadata>
Name: Year-in-Review
Category: personal
Aliases: Annual Personal Retro, End-of-Year Review, Personal Annual Review, Life Audit
Duration: 2–4 hours total (split across 2–3 sessions)
Team Size: 1 (individual practice)
Best For: Full personal retrospective across all life dimensions; celebrating growth, processing challenges, and setting intentions for the coming year
Definition: A full personal retrospective conducted annually to review all dimensions of life and work over the past year, celebrate growth, process challenges, and set intentions for the coming year.
</Metadata>

<Gotchas>
1. Use when: Annually, typically December–January (natural transition point); At major life transitions (career change, relocation, relationship change); Milestone birthdays or anniversaries that invite deep reflection; When feeling directionless and needing to reconnect with purpose
2. Do NOT use when: When in active crisis (need support, not reflection; seek help first); Monthly (too much scope for frequent use ;  use weekly personal retro instead); When it would become a guilt exercise rather than a growth exercise
3. Goal-list disguised as reflection: Jumping straight to next year's goals without actually reflecting on the year behind. Fix: Spend 70% of time looking back, 30% looking forward. Understanding the past IS the foundation for wise future intentions.
4. Work-only review: Reviewing only professional accomplishments while ignoring relationships, health, growth, and meaning. Fix: Explicitly include ALL life dimensions. You're reviewing a year of LIFE, not just a year of work.
5. Perfectionism or comparison: Measuring the year against unrealistic standards or other people's highlight reels. Fix: Compare yourself to yourself 12 months ago. That's the only valid comparison.
6. Too many intentions: Setting 15 goals/intentions for next year (guaranteeing overwhelm and failure). Fix: Maximum 5 intentions. If everything is a priority, nothing is.
7. Marathon session: Attempting 4 hours in one sitting and burning out halfway. Fix: Split across 2–3 sessions over several days. Let insights from Session 1 percolate before Session 2.
</Gotchas>

<Instructions>
<Workflow - Year-in-Review
  description="Step-by-step facilitation guide for Year-in-Review."
>
1. [Setup] (Week before)
   - Block 2–3 sessions of 60–90 minutes each (don't try to do 4 hours straight)
   - Gather evidence from the year: calendar, photos, journal entries, brag sheet, social media timeline, email
   - Choose a reflective space: comfortable, private, unhurried
   - Print or open your review template
   - Optional: review last year's intentions to see what manifested

2. [Open] (Session 1; 5 minutes)
   - Take a few deep breaths. This is about honoring the year that was; all of it.
   - Set intention: "I'm here to look back honestly, celebrate fully, and look forward wisely"
   - If you have last year's review/intentions, open them for reference
   - Note your emotional state as you begin

3. [Stage: Accomplishments & Wins] (30 minutes)
   - Purpose: Celebrate what was achieved across all life areas (not just work)
   - Prompt: "What did I accomplish this year? In career, relationships, health, learning, creative pursuits, finances, personal growth? What am I proud of?"
   - Facilitation tip: Go month by month using your calendar/photos as memory aids. Include ALL domains, professional wins, relationship milestones, health achievements, creative output, learning completed, adventures taken. Don't filter by size, small wins count. Combat the human tendency to forget your own growth.

4. [Stage: Challenges & Difficulties] (25 minutes)
   - Purpose: Acknowledge what was hard without judgment - honor the struggle
   - Prompt: "What was hard this year? What challenged me, hurt me, or tested me? What didn't go as planned? What loss or disappointment did I experience?"
   - Facilitation tip: This isn't about self-criticism. It's about honest acknowledgment. Some challenges were external (things that happened TO you); some were internal (patterns you struggled with). Both deserve recognition. "This was hard" is a complete and valid statement.

5. [Stage: Lessons Learned] (25 minutes; Session 2)
   - Purpose: Extract wisdom from both successes and struggles
   - Prompt: "What did I learn about myself this year? About others? About the world? What assumptions were proven wrong? What truths were confirmed?"
   - Facilitation tip: Lessons come from BOTH wins and challenges. "I learned that I thrive with structure" (from a win). "I learned that I avoid conflict until it explodes" (from a challenge). Look for the deeper insight beneath the surface event. What did this year teach you that you didn't know 12 months ago?

6. [Stage: Relationships & Connections] (15 minutes)
   - Purpose: Reflect on how key relationships evolved and how your social world changed
   - Prompt: "How did my key relationships evolve this year? Who entered my life? Who drifted away? Where did I invest well? Where did I underinvest?"
   - Facilitation tip: Relationships are often the most meaningful dimension but the least systematically reflected upon. Consider: partner, family, close friends, professional relationships, community. Note both gratitude and regret without judgment.

7. [Stage: Growth & Identity] (15 minutes)
   - Purpose: Recognize how you've changed as a person over 12 months
   - Prompt: "How am I different from the person I was in January? What's changed in my values, perspectives, capabilities, or character? What have I outgrown?"
   - Facilitation tip: Growth is often invisible day-to-day but stark year-over-year. Compare: What scared you in January that doesn't now? What do you believe now that you didn't then? What can you do now that you couldn't? This stage builds identity continuity and self-knowledge.

8. [Stage: Gratitude & Meaning] (10 minutes)
   - Purpose: Anchor the review in appreciation and meaning-making
   - Prompt: "What am I most grateful for this year? What gave my life meaning? What moments would I want to remember forever?"
   - Facilitation tip: Gratitude counterbalances the natural negativity bias in self-reflection. Be specific: not "I'm grateful for my health" but "I'm grateful that I could run the 10K in May after thinking I'd never run again."

9. [Stage: Themes & Word of the Year] (10 minutes)
   - Purpose: Synthesize the year into a defining narrative
   - Prompt: "If this year had a title, what would it be? What one word or phrase captures the essence of these 12 months?"
   - Facilitation tip: This is narrative sense-making; turning 365 days of events into a story with meaning. The theme might be: "The year I rebuilt," "Expansion," "Letting go," "Foundation." This word becomes how you'll remember and reference this year.

10. [Stage: Intentions for Next Year] (20 minutes; Session 3)
    - Purpose: Set directional intentions (not rigid goals) for the coming year
    - Prompt: "What do I want for next year? Not a to-do list, but a direction. What themes, focuses, or intentions do I want to carry forward? What do I want MORE of? LESS of?"
    - Facilitation tip: Limit to 3–5 intentions. Frame as directions rather than specific targets: "Deepen close friendships" rather than "Have dinner with 3 friends per month." Intentions create a compass; goals create a checklist. Both have value but annual intentions work better at the life-design level.

11. [Synthesize] (10 minutes)
    - Read through the complete review
    - Note any actions that emerged (conversations to have, changes to make)
    - File the review where you can find it next December

12. [Close] (5 minutes)
    - Appreciate yourself for the practice of honest self-reflection
    - Note one feeling: how do you feel about the year behind? The year ahead?
    - Close the document. Take a walk. Let it integrate.
</Workflow - Year-in-Review>

<Resources>
- Use your calendar and photos as memory aids. Walk through month by month; you'll be surprised what you've forgotten. The brain is terrible at remembering growth because it normalizes current state.
- Spread the review across 2–3 days. Session 1: looking back (accomplishments, challenges). Session 2: extracting meaning (lessons, growth, gratitude). Session 3: looking forward (intentions). Sleep between sessions allows integration.
- Include the emotional dimension. A year that looks "productive" on paper might have felt hollow. A year that looks "unsuccessful" might have contained profound growth. Honor feeling alongside achievement.
- If you did this last year, start by reviewing your previous intentions. Which manifested? Which didn't? What does that teach you about what you actually prioritize vs. what you think you should?
- Consider sharing a curated version with a partner, close friend, or mentor - not the full private document, but the themes and intentions. Sharing creates accountability and connection.
- The "Year Theme" exercise is surprisingly powerful. Naming the year creates narrative coherence. "The year I said yes" or "The year I rebuilt" gives 365 disconnected days a story arc that supports meaning-making.
</Resources>
