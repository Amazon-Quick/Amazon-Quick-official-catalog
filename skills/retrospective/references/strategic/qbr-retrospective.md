# QBR Retrospective

<Metadata>
Name: QBR Retrospective
Category: strategic
Aliases: Quarterly Business Review, Quarterly Review, Business Review, Quarterly Planning Retro
Duration: 2–3 hours
Team Size: 5–15
Best For: Leadership teams reviewing quarterly performance and connecting execution insights to next quarter's strategy
Definition: A structured quarterly session where leadership teams review the previous quarter's performance, examine what drove or blocked results, and use findings to inform next quarter's strategy.
</Metadata>

<Gotchas>
1. Use when: End of each quarter as part of the planning cadence; When connecting execution results to strategic direction; When leadership team needs alignment on what's working and what isn't; Before annual planning cycles (Q3/Q4 QBRs inform annual planning); When departments are operating in silos and need cross-functional insight
2. Do NOT use when: Weekly or sprint-level reflection (too infrequent for operational teams); When no meaningful data exists (new business unit in first quarter); Purely tactical teams without strategic scope; When leadership isn't prepared to make decisions based on findings
3. Present and forget: Numbers are shown, but no decisions are made and no learning is extracted. The QBR becomes a performance theater. Fix: End every topic with "So what's the decision?" Track decision count. If zero decisions, the QBR failed.
4. Only reviewing failures: Spending all time on misses and ignoring wins. Successes have equally valuable drivers to understand. Fix: Dedicate equal time to wins and misses. Ask "Why did this work?" with the same rigor as "Why did this fail?"
5. Data without insight: Presenting 50 slides of metrics without ever asking "why?" or "so what?" Fix: Limit data presentation to 30 minutes max. The majority of time should be discussion and decision-making.
6. No follow-through: Decisions made in the QBR are forgotten by week 2 of the next quarter. Fix: Start next QBR by reviewing previous QBR decisions and their outcomes.
7. Seniority silencing: Junior leaders self-censor in front of executives. Fix: Use silent writing before discussion. Explicitly invite dissenting views. Consider pre-work surveys to surface issues anonymously.
</Gotchas>

<Instructions>
<Workflow - QBR Retrospective
  description="Step-by-step facilitation guide for QBR Retrospective."
>
1. [Setup] (1–2 weeks prior)
   - Compile quarterly data package: revenue vs. target, key metrics, OKR scores, customer satisfaction, pipeline, team metrics
   - Send data package to all participants 5+ days in advance with instruction: "Review data and come prepared to discuss why results occurred, not just what they were"
   - Prepare quarter-over-quarter trend visualizations
   - Identify 3–5 "discussion topics," areas where results deviated significantly from expectations (positive or negative)
   - Book sufficient time (2.5–3 hours) and confirm all decision makers can attend

2. [Open] (15 minutes)
   - State purpose: "We're here to understand WHY our quarter played out as it did, and to use that understanding to make better decisions for next quarter"
   - Frame the retrospective distinction: "This isn't just a readout; it's a learning session"
   - Confirm the ground rules: data-driven, honest assessment, decisions will be made today

3. [Stage: Results Review] (30 minutes)
   - Purpose: Establish shared factual understanding of what happened
   - Prompt: "Let's walk through the numbers. Where did we beat expectations? Where did we miss? What surprised us?"
   - Facilitation tip: Present data visually: trend lines, target vs. actual, quarter-over-quarter comparison. Keep this factual. Resist interpretation yet. Mark items for deeper discussion. Flag both positive and negative surprises.

4. [Stage: Driver Analysis - Wins] (30 minutes)
   - Purpose: Understand root causes of success so they can be amplified
   - Prompt: "For our biggest wins this quarter, what drove them? What decisions, market conditions, or team actions made these results possible? Are they repeatable?"
   - Facilitation tip: Push beyond "the team worked hard." Ask: What specific investments, decisions, or market timing created the win? Would this happen again next quarter or was it one-time? This determines what to double down on.

5. [Stage: Driver Analysis - Misses] (30 minutes)
   - Purpose: Identify root causes of underperformance without blame
   - Prompt: "For areas where we missed, what happened? Were our assumptions wrong? Our execution? External factors? What would we need to change to get a different result?"
   - Facilitation tip: Distinguish between: (a) wrong strategy, (b) right strategy/poor execution, (c) unforeseeable external factors. Each requires a different response. Use blameless inquiry. Focus on system failures, not individual failures.

6. [Stage: Strategic Lessons] (30 minutes)
   - Purpose: Extract meta-insights about strategy, market, and organizational capabilities
   - Prompt: "Stepping back from individual metrics, what did this quarter teach us about our strategy? Our market? Our capabilities? What assumptions have been validated or invalidated?"
   - Facilitation tip: This is the highest-value stage and the most often skipped. Push for strategic-level insights: "The market is moving faster than we planned for," "Our capacity to execute multi-quarter initiatives is weaker than assumed," "Customer segment X is more receptive than expected."

7. [Stage: Decisions for Next Quarter] (30 minutes)
   - Purpose: Convert insights into specific strategic and operational decisions
   - Prompt: "Based on what we've learned, what will we do differently next quarter? What will we double down on? What will we stop or redirect? What resource changes are needed?"
   - Facilitation tip: Force decisions, not just observations. End each discussion thread with "So what's the decision?" Document each decision with owner, timeline, and success criteria. Connect decisions explicitly to next quarter's OKRs.

8. [Synthesize] (15 minutes)
   - Review all decisions made during the session
   - Confirm owners and timelines for each
   - Identify what needs to be communicated to broader organization
   - Note any unresolved items that need further analysis

9. [Close] (10 minutes)
   - Read back the decisions list
   - Confirm next steps: who drafts next quarter OKRs, who communicates changes
   - Quick reflection: "How was this QBR different from a typical status readout? What should we keep for next time?"
   - Schedule next QBR
</Workflow - QBR Retrospective>
</Instructions>

<Resources>
- The most common failure mode is "QBR as presentation." Fight this by limiting data presentation to 20% of the session and dedicating 80% to discussion and decisions.
- Pre-distribute the data package at least 5 days early. If people haven't reviewed it, you'll waste session time on information transfer. Consider canceling if pre-work wasn't done.
- The "Strategic Lessons" stage is the unique value of adding retrospective thinking to a QBR. Standard QBRs review numbers and set targets. The retrospective lens asks "What did this quarter teach us about our strategy?" This is where the real value lives.
- For multi-department QBRs, consider 1-hour department-level pre-sessions that feed into the cross-functional QBR. This prevents the main session from becoming a parade of department presentations.
- End with explicit "What will we STOP doing?" QBRs typically add new initiatives but rarely remove existing ones. Force the subtraction conversation.
</Resources>
