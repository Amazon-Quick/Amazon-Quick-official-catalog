# Annual Planning Retrospective

<Metadata>
Name: Annual Planning Retrospective
Category: strategic
Aliases: Year-End Business Review, Annual Debrief, Planning Cycle Retro
Duration: Half-day to full day
Team Size: 6–20
Best For: Full review of the outgoing year that directly feeds into next year's annual planning process
Definition: A complete review of the outgoing year's performance, plans, and decisions that directly feeds into next year's annual planning process.
</Metadata>

<Gotchas>
1. Use when: Annually, in Q4 before next year's planning begins; When annual planning cycles feel disconnected from reality; After a year with significant strategic surprises; When organizational learning across the year needs to be synthesized; Before committing resources to next year's priorities
2. Do NOT use when: Mid-year (too early to judge annual plans. Use QBRs instead); Organizations without formal annual planning processes; When the year is still in active execution and outcomes are unclear; As a replacement for quarterly retrospectives (different scope)
3. Planning retro IS the planning: Conflating the retrospective with the annual planning session itself. Fix: These are separate activities. The retro INFORMS planning but doesn't replace it. Schedule them in sequence with a gap between.
4. Revisionist history: Rewriting what was planned to make actuals look better. "We always knew we'd pivot." Fix: Document the original plan precisely and compare honestly. Review the actual plan documents from January.
5. Only looking forward: Skipping the retrospective to jump straight into next year's planning because "we don't have time." Fix: An hour of honest retrospection prevents repeating mistakes worth months of effort.
6. Data-free opinions: Debating what happened without data. Fix: Compile the data package weeks in advance. Base discussion on evidence, not memory (which is unreliable over 12 months).
7. No planning process review: Reviewing business results but never examining whether the planning process itself is effective. Fix: Dedicate at least 30 minutes specifically to "how we plan" separate from "what happened."
</Gotchas>

<Instructions>
<Workflow - Annual Planning Retrospective
  description="Step-by-step facilitation guide for Annual Planning Retrospective."
>
1. [Setup] (2–3 weeks prior)
   - Compile annual data: financial performance, OKR scores by quarter, employee survey results, customer satisfaction trends, market share data
   - Document original annual plan: goals, resource allocations, strategic bets, key initiatives
   - Calculate plan vs. actual across all major dimensions
   - Send pre-work: "Reflect on the year. What was the biggest surprise? What do we know now that we wish we'd known in January?"
   - Schedule multiple inputs: finance data, HR data, customer data, market data
   - Consider pre-session interviews with key leaders

2. [Open] (20 minutes)
   - Frame purpose: "We're doing two things today: (1) Understanding what happened this year and why, and (2) Improving how we PLAN so next year's plan is better"
   - Present the original annual plan alongside actual outcomes, visual comparison
   - Set ground rules: honest assessment, no revisionist history, learning-oriented
   - Acknowledge the challenge: "It's hard to remember January's context from December's perspective. That's why we have data."

3. [Stage: Plan vs. Actual] (40 minutes)
   - Purpose: Understand the gap between what was planned and what actually happened
   - Prompt: "Looking at our annual plan vs. actual results, where were we right? Where were we wrong? And most importantly, why?"
   - Facilitation tip: Use visual comparisons: planned revenue vs. actual, planned headcount vs. actual, planned timeline vs. actual for key initiatives. Don't just note the gaps. Categorize WHY: poor estimation, scope change, market shift, execution issue, or deliberate pivot.

4. [Stage: Key Achievements] (25 minutes)
   - Purpose: Celebrate and understand what drove the year's major wins
   - Prompt: "What were our biggest wins this year? Which were planned and which were unexpected? What conditions or decisions enabled them?"
   - Facilitation tip: Include wins that WEREN'T in the plan. Opportunistic successes are as instructive as planned ones. Ask "Would this win have happened with a different plan?" to understand the relationship between planning and outcomes.

5. [Stage: Missed Targets] (30 minutes)
   - Purpose: Understand what didn't land and extract learning from misses
   - Prompt: "Where did we fall short of our ambitions? For each miss, was the goal wrong, the execution poor, or did something unpredictable intervene?"
   - Facilitation tip: Categorize misses: (a) goal was unrealistic from the start, (b) goal was right but execution faltered, (c) external factors made the goal irrelevant, (d) priorities shifted deliberately. Each category has a different implication for next year's planning.

6. [Stage: Planning Process Review] (30 minutes)
   - Purpose: Improve how the organization plans (meta-level, how do we plan better?)
   - Prompt: "Was our planning process effective? Was the plan too detailed or too vague? Did it survive contact with reality? What would make our planning process better next year?"
   - Facilitation tip: This is the uniquely valuable stage. Questions to explore: Was the plan at the right altitude (too detailed = brittle, too vague = directionless)? Did the plan help decision-making during the year? Were quarterly adjustments adequate? Did resource allocation match actual needs?

7. [Stage: External Surprises] (20 minutes)
   - Purpose: Build humility into planning by acknowledging unpredictability
   - Prompt: "What couldn't have been predicted? What surprised us? How quickly did we adapt? What does this tell us about next year's planning?"
   - Facilitation tip: This stage builds planning humility, acknowledging that plans are hypotheses, not prophecies. Identify which surprises were truly unpredictable vs. which were predictable but ignored. Build "scenario buffers" and contingency thinking into next year's process.

8. [Stage: Inputs for Next Year] (30 minutes)
   - Purpose: Generate specific inputs that must inform next year's planning
   - Prompt: "Based on everything we've discussed, what MUST inform next year's plan? What constraints, opportunities, lessons, and assumptions should next year's planners know?"
   - Facilitation tip: Create a formal "input document" for next year's planning. Include: validated assumptions, invalidated assumptions, capability gaps discovered, market truths learned, planning process improvements. This document becomes required reading for the planning team.

9. [Synthesize] (20 minutes)
   - Summarize the year's narrative: what happened, why, and what we learned
   - Confirm planning process improvements for next cycle
   - Finalize the inputs document for annual planning
   - Assign ownership for documentation and distribution

10. [Close] (15 minutes)
    - Acknowledge the year's collective effort, regardless of results
    - Confirm timeline for annual planning kickoff
    - Final round: "One word to describe this year. One word for your aspiration for next year."
    - Express gratitude for honest participation
</Workflow - Annual Planning Retrospective>
</Instructions>

<Resources>
- The 12-month timescale makes specific memory unreliable. Data is essential: financial reports, quarterly OKR scores, employee survey results, key dates, and decisions made. Without data, the discussion becomes anecdotal.
- Separate "business results review" from "planning process review" explicitly. The first is about what happened; the second is about how to plan better. Both are valuable but require different thinking modes.
- Include "what surprised us" deliberately. This builds humility into planning, acknowledging that the future is uncertain and plans are hypotheses. Organizations that plan with certainty are fragile; those that plan with adaptability are resilient.
- Consider splitting across 2 days: Day 1 for looking back (results, achievements, misses, surprises), Day 2 for looking forward (process improvements, inputs for next year). This prevents fatigue from undermining the forward-looking conversation.
- The output document ("Input to Next Year's Planning") is the primary deliverable. If no one reads it during annual planning, the retrospective was wasted. Assign it a formal role in the planning process, e.g., it's the first document reviewed at planning kickoff.
- For large organizations, consider a cascade: department-level annual retros feeding into an executive synthesis. This provides richer data than a single executive-only session.
</Resources>
