# Strategy Retrospective

<Metadata>
Name: Strategy Retrospective
Category: strategic
Aliases: Strategic Review, Strategy Reflection, Annual Strategy Retro
Duration: Half-day to full day
Team Size: 5–15
Best For: Executive-level examination of whether organizational strategy remains valid and what pivots may be needed
Definition: A high-level retrospective examining whether the organization's strategic direction remains valid, what assumptions have been proven or disproven, and what strategic pivots may be needed.
</Metadata>

<Gotchas>
1. Use when: Annual planning cycle input (typically Q3/Q4); After major market disruptions, competitive moves, or industry shifts; When strategy execution gaps persist across multiple quarters; After M&A, reorganizations, or major organizational changes; When leadership suspects the strategy set 12+ months ago may no longer be valid
2. Do NOT use when: Operational or tactical teams (use project or sprint retros instead); Too frequently. Strategy needs time to execute before being judged (minimum 6 months); When leadership isn't genuinely prepared to change course; When the problem is clearly execution, not strategy direction
3. Strategy by inertia: Reviewing strategy without willingness to change it. The retro confirms existing direction regardless of evidence. Fix: Assign a devil's advocate. Ask "What would need to be true for us to ABANDON this strategy?" If those conditions exist, act.
4. Only reviewing failures: Focusing on what didn't work while ignoring successful strategies that may need validation. Fix: Explicitly validate successful strategies. "Is this still working because of our strategy or despite it?"
5. No decisions made: Interesting intellectual discussion that doesn't result in actual strategic changes. Fix: End with explicit decisions. Track: "What did we change as a result of this session?" If nothing, the session failed.
6. Echo chamber: Only internal voices, confirming internal beliefs. Fix: Bring external data: customer research, analyst reports, competitive intelligence. Consider inviting external advisors for portions of the session.
7. Too frequent: Reviewing strategy quarterly dilutes execution focus. Strategy needs time to work before being judged. Fix: Annual or bi-annual cadence maximum. Use QBRs for operational review.
</Gotchas>

<Instructions>
<Workflow - Strategy Retrospective
  description="Step-by-step facilitation guide for Strategy Retrospective."
>
1. [Setup] (2–3 weeks prior)
   - Compile data package: market analysis, competitive intelligence, internal performance data, customer research, industry trends
   - Document the original strategic assumptions (from when strategy was set)
   - Gather input from customer-facing teams, product teams, and market research
   - Identify external speakers or data sources that challenge current thinking
   - Send pre-read package 10+ days in advance: "Come prepared to challenge our assumptions"
   - Consider 360° input: board members, customers, partners, frontline employees

2. [Open] (20 minutes)
   - State purpose: "We're here to test whether our strategic direction is still right, and to have the courage to change if it's not"
   - Read back the original strategy statement and the assumptions it was built on
   - Set ground rules: evidence-based, intellectually honest, status/politics left at the door
   - Acknowledge: "It takes courage to question a strategy we've invested in. That's exactly what we're here to do."

3. [Stage: Assumption Review] (45 minutes)
   - Purpose: Test which strategic assumptions still hold and which have been invalidated by reality
   - Prompt: "When we set this strategy, we assumed [X, Y, Z]. Looking at the evidence now, which of these assumptions have been validated? Which have been proven wrong? Which are still uncertain?"
   - Facilitation tip: List each assumption explicitly. For each one, ask "What evidence supports this still being true? What evidence contradicts it?" Force intellectual honesty. Confirmation bias is the enemy. Actively seek disconfirming evidence. Consider assigning a "red team" member.

4. [Stage: External Environment Scan] (30 minutes)
   - Purpose: Identify what has changed externally since strategy was set
   - Prompt: "What has changed in our market, competitive landscape, technology environment, regulatory context, or customer needs since we set this strategy?"
   - Facilitation tip: Bring external data. Don't rely solely on internal perception. Customer research, competitive moves, market reports, technology shifts. The team is often the last to notice external change because they're focused on execution.

5. [Stage: Execution Assessment] (30 minutes)
   - Purpose: Evaluate where execution matched or deviated from strategy, and whether deviations were adaptive or accidental
   - Prompt: "Where did our execution align with strategy? Where did it deviate? For deviations, were they intentional adaptations or accidental drift?"
   - Facilitation tip: Distinguish between "we chose to pivot" and "we drifted without deciding." Intentional pivots suggest strategy is being updated in real-time (good). Accidental drift suggests the strategy isn't guiding decisions (bad). Both are important signals.

6. [Stage: Capability Gap Analysis] (30 minutes)
   - Purpose: Identify capabilities the organization lacks for its strategic ambitions
   - Prompt: "What capabilities does our strategy require that we don't yet have? What capabilities have we developed that our strategy didn't anticipate needing?"
   - Facilitation tip: Be honest about gaps. "We'll figure it out" is not a strategy. Categorize gaps: build vs. buy vs. partner. New capabilities that emerged unexpectedly may signal opportunities for strategy adjustment.

7. [Stage: Strategic Bets Review] (30 minutes)
   - Purpose: Evaluate which strategic bets are paying off and which should be doubled down, maintained, or abandoned
   - Prompt: "Which of our strategic bets have evidence of working? Which are showing early warning signs of failure? What's our decision on each: double down, maintain course, or cut?"
   - Facilitation tip: This is the hardest stage; it requires killing things people are invested in. Use the evidence-based framework: "What would need to be true for this bet to pay off? Is that still plausible?" Sunk cost is not a reason to continue.

8. [Stage: Direction Setting] (30 minutes)
   - Purpose: Define what strategic adjustments are needed based on the day's analysis
   - Prompt: "Based on everything we've examined today, what strategic adjustments do we need to make? What doubles down? What pivots? What gets killed? What new directions emerge?"
   - Facilitation tip: Force decisions. The common failure mode is "interesting discussion, no changes." Explicitly ask: "Are we changing the strategy or confirming it?" Both are valid outcomes but must be explicit. Document decisions with clear owners.

9. [Synthesize] (20 minutes)
   - Review all decisions and strategic adjustments
   - Confirm updated strategy statement (if changed)
   - Identify communication plan: who needs to know, how, and when
   - Assign ownership for implementing changes

10. [Close] (15 minutes)
    - Read the updated/confirmed strategy direction
    - Confirm follow-up actions and owners
    - Reflect: "What did it take for us to have this conversation honestly? How do we maintain that going forward?"
    - Schedule next strategy retrospective
</Workflow - Strategy Retrospective>
</Instructions>

<Resources>
- This is one of the few retrospective formats that benefits from an external facilitator, possibly a board advisor, strategy consultant, or executive coach. Internal facilitators may pull punches with senior leadership.
- The assumption validation exercise is the single most valuable activity. Organizations rarely explicitly test their assumptions. Write them down at strategy-setting time specifically so they can be tested later.
- Challenge confirmation bias aggressively. For every assumption "validated," ask: "What evidence would change your mind?" If no evidence could, you're dealing with belief, not strategy.
- Include external perspective: customer voice, market data, competitive intelligence. The further from the customer a leadership team sits, the more likely they are to miss environmental changes.
- The "strategic bets review" requires explicit kill criteria defined in advance. Without predefined failure conditions, sunk cost fallacy will keep failing bets alive indefinitely. "What would need to happen for us to stop this?"
- Consider splitting into two sessions: Day 1 for analysis (assumptions, external scan, execution review), Day 2 for decisions (bets review, direction setting). Allows overnight reflection before major decisions.
</Resources>
