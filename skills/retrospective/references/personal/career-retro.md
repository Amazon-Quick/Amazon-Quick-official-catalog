# Career Retrospective

<Metadata>
Name: Career Retrospective
Category: personal
Aliases: Career Check-in, Professional Development Retro, Role Retro, Professional Growth Review
Duration: 45–60 minutes
Team Size: 1 (individual practice)
Best For: Periodic examination of whether daily work aligns with long-term career goals, and identifying intentional growth actions
Definition: A periodic (quarterly or semi-annual) personal retrospective focused specifically on career growth, skill development, and professional trajectory.
</Metadata>

<Gotchas>
1. Use when: Quarterly as a regular career health check; At role transitions (first 90 days, approaching promotion, post-promotion); When feeling stagnant or professionally restless; Before 1:1s with your manager about career development; When preparing for promotions or role changes; After receiving feedback (positive or constructive) that prompts growth reflection
2. Do NOT use when: When in immediate job crisis (address survival first, reflect later); Weekly cadence (too frequent; career trajectory is visible over months, not days); When the issue is environmental not personal (toxic culture → leave, don't self-optimize)
3. Only when unhappy: Only doing career reflection when feeling stuck or dissatisfied. Fix: Proactive quarterly check-ins prevent stagnation before it sets in. The best time to steer is when you still have momentum.
4. Skills without application: Identifying skill gaps and completing courses without ever applying the new skill. Fix: Every skill gap action must include a real-world application opportunity: a project, a volunteer task, a stretch assignment.
5. Ignoring the energy signal: Seeing persistent low energy for your current work type but not adjusting trajectory. Fix: Energy is data about fit. Persistent misalignment between what energizes you and what your role requires is a signal to explore different paths - not to push harder.
6. All analysis, no action: Detailed self-assessment that doesn't result in concrete next steps. Fix: End every career retro with 2–3 specific, time-bound actions. If you can't name an action, the insight hasn't been operationalized.
7. Comparison spiral: Using the career retro to compare yourself unfavorably to peers. Fix: Compare yourself to yourself 3 months ago. That's the only valid benchmark for a career retro.
</Gotchas>

<Instructions>
<Workflow - Career Retrospective
  description="Step-by-step facilitation guide for Career Retrospective."
>
1. [Setup] (Before session)
   - Pull up your role/level expectations document (or promotion criteria for next level)
   - Review your brag sheet or recent accomplishments
   - Open your previous career retro (if exists) to check on past intentions
   - Have your career aspirations accessible (where do you WANT to go?)

2. [Open] (3 minutes)
   - Ground yourself: "I'm here to honestly assess my career trajectory - not to judge myself, but to steer intentionally"
   - Review: Where did I say I wanted to be heading? (check previous retro)
   - Note current emotional state about career (satisfied / restless / stuck / energized)

3. [Stage: Skills Inventory] (10 minutes)
   - Purpose: Assess what capabilities you've built, maintained, and lost over the recent period
   - Prompt: "What new skills did I develop this quarter? What skills did I use heavily? What skills are atrophying from disuse? Where did I stretch beyond my comfort zone?"
   - Facilitation tip: Be specific about skills - not "leadership" but "influencing cross-team decisions without authority." Note both technical and professional skills. Track atrophy honestly; skills you're not using are fading, and that's data about your current role's growth potential.

4. [Stage: Alignment Check] (10 minutes)
   - Purpose: Assess whether daily work is moving you toward your career aspirations
   - Prompt: "Is my daily work aligned with where I want to go? What percentage of my time is spent on growth-aligned activities vs. maintenance? If I keep doing exactly what I'm doing now for 2 more years, where do I end up?"
   - Facilitation tip: The "2-year projection" question is powerful. If the answer is "exactly where I am now," that's stagnation data. If it's "where I want to be," great; stay the course. If it's "somewhere I don't want to be," that's urgent signal to change. Be honest about inertia vs. intention.

5. [Stage: Network Health] (10 minutes)
   - Purpose: Assess professional relationships, visibility, and connections
   - Prompt: "How are my professional relationships? Who knows about my work and aspirations? Who should I connect with? Am I visible to decision-makers?"
   - Facilitation tip: Career advancement requires visibility and relationships, not just output. Assess: Does your manager know your aspirations? Do skip-level leaders know you? Are you connected to people in your desired future role? Are you learning from people ahead of you? Identify specific relationship investments to make.

6. [Stage: Market Relevance] (5 minutes)
   - Purpose: Ensure capabilities remain valuable in the broader market
   - Prompt: "Am I developing capabilities that the market values? If I left my current role, would I be competitive? What skills are the market increasingly demanding?"
   - Facilitation tip: Even if you're not planning to leave, market-relevance is insurance and bargaining power. Look at job postings for roles you'd want. What do they require? Where are you strong? Where are the gaps? This prevents being locked into a single organization's needs.

7. [Stage: Energy Audit] (5 minutes)
   - Purpose: Assess which aspects of work give energy versus deplete it
   - Prompt: "What work gives me energy and flow? What depletes me? Is the ratio improving or worsening? What does this tell me about the right role/trajectory?"
   - Facilitation tip: Energy data is career direction data. Consistent energy from technical deep work suggests IC track. Energy from enabling others suggests management/leadership. Energy from ambiguity suggests strategy/entrepreneurship. Don't ignore this signal.

8. [Stage: Gap Analysis & Actions] (10 minutes)
   - Purpose: Identify specific capabilities needed for your next career step and plan to build them
   - Prompt: "What capabilities do I need for my next role/level that I don't have yet? What's the most impactful skill gap to close this quarter? What specific action will I take?"
   - Facilitation tip: Limit to 1–2 gaps per quarter. For each, define: the gap, why it matters for your next step, and a specific action (take on a stretch project, find a mentor, complete a course, volunteer for a cross-team initiative). Put it in your sprint planning.

9. [Synthesize] (5 minutes)
   - Summarize: alignment status, top skill gap, key relationship to invest in, energy pattern
   - Record 2–3 specific actions for the next quarter
   - Note any conversations needed (with manager, mentor, or peers)

10. [Close] (2 minutes)
    - Appreciate your intentionality - most people drift rather than steer
    - Confirm: "My top career action for next quarter is [X]"
    - Set next career retro date (quarterly)
</Workflow - Career Retrospective>
</Instructions>

<Resources>
- The "2-year projection" question is the single most powerful tool in this retro. If you keep doing exactly what you're doing, where do you end up? If the answer excites you, stay the course. If it doesn't, this quarter is the time to change trajectory.
- Career growth isn't linear - it has seasons. Sometimes you're in a "growth sprint" (steep learning curve). Sometimes you're in a "harvest season" (applying existing skills at high impact). Sometimes you're in a "transition" (deliberately building toward a pivot). Know which season you're in.
- Be honest about alignment - many people discover their daily work has drifted far from their stated aspirations without a conscious decision to change direction. This isn't failure; it's valuable awareness.
- Network/visibility assessment is uncomfortable but critical. "If the promotion committee met tomorrow, would they know my work?" is a gut-check worth having quarterly.
- Consider doing this retro WITH a trusted mentor, coach, or peer for accountability and external perspective. They may see strengths, gaps, or patterns you can't see yourself.
</Resources>
