# Energy Levels Retrospective

<Metadata>
Name: Energy Levels Retrospective
Category: team-health
Aliases: Energy Retro, Battery Levels, What Energizes/Drains, Motivation Retro
Duration: 30–45 minutes
Team Size: 3–10
Best For: Understanding what gives team members energy versus what drains them; revealing burnout risk and enabling task redistribution
Definition: A retrospective focused on understanding what gives team members energy (motivation, excitement, flow states) and what drains them (burnout signals, tedious tasks, frustrations).
</Metadata>

<Gotchas>
1. Use when: When team engagement seems low despite tasks getting completed; After intense delivery periods (burnout risk assessment); When retention is a concern and you want to understand what's draining people; Quarterly wellness check-in as part of team health cadence; When you notice team members seem disengaged but can't articulate why; Before task allocation discussions (align work with energy)
2. Do NOT use when: When process improvement is the primary goal (this is about people, not process); When team has urgent delivery pressure and the retro would feel like a luxury; Weekly cadence (too frequent; energy patterns need time to emerge); When energy data might be weaponized ("they have energy to spare, give them more!")
3. Weaponizing energy data: Using the insight that someone has energy as justification for loading them up with more work. Fix: Energy data informs TYPE of work, not VOLUME. Protect people's energizers; don't exploit them.
4. Only focusing on drains: Spending the entire session on what's draining without celebrating energizers. Fix: Start with high-energy items. Acknowledge and protect the energizing work; it's what keeps people going.
5. Drains as permanent: Accepting all draining work as inevitable. Fix: Challenge every drain: Can it be eliminated? Automated? Redistributed? Timeboxed? Don't assume the current distribution is the only option.
6. Skipping the individual level: Only looking at team-level patterns without checking on individual energy. Fix: Someone might be deeply depleted while the team average looks fine. Watch for outliers.
7. One-time event: Running energy check once without establishing a cadence. Fix: Quarterly minimum. Energy patterns shift with project phases, seasons, and life changes.
</Gotchas>

<Instructions>
<Workflow - Energy Levels Retrospective
  description="Step-by-step facilitation guide for Energy Levels Retrospective."
>
1. [Setup] (Before session)
   - Draw or prepare a battery visualization with three levels: Full ⚡, Half 🔋, Empty 🪫
   - Optionally add a fourth: "What would recharge us?" 🔌
   - Prepare recent task/activity list for reference (what the team worked on recently)
   - Ensure a safe, private environment; energy conversations touch on vulnerability

2. [Open] (5 minutes)
   - Frame purpose: "We're exploring what TYPE of work energizes versus drains us - not how MUCH work. This helps us distribute work more intelligently and spot burnout early."
   - Normalize: "It's okay to be drained by work others love. It's okay to love work others find tedious. Diversity of energy sources is healthy."
   - Quick battery check: "Right now, where's your overall energy level? Full/Half/Empty?" (sets baseline)

3. [Stage: Energy Mapping] (15 minutes)
   - Purpose: Categorize recent activities and tasks by their energy impact
   - Prompt: "Looking at the past [sprint/month] - sort your activities into three categories: What GAVE you energy (full battery), what was neutral (half battery), and what DRAINED you (empty battery)."
   - Facilitation tip: Use silent writing first (5–7 minutes). One item per sticky note placed in the appropriate battery zone. Then share in round-robin. Look for patterns: Is the same activity in different zones for different people? That's gold; it means you can redistribute.

4. [Stage: Pattern Recognition] (10 minutes)
   - Purpose: Identify systemic patterns - types of work that consistently energize or drain
   - Prompt: "Looking at everyone's inputs - what patterns do you see? Are certain types of work universally draining? Are there energy mismatches where one person's drain is another's fuel?"
   - Facilitation tip: Look for: (a) Universal drains (affect everyone - these are process problems), (b) Individual drains (affect specific people - redistribution opportunity), (c) Energy mismatches (A hates what B loves - swap opportunity). Mark each pattern type differently.

5. [Stage: Rebalancing Discussion] (10 minutes)
   - Purpose: Explore concrete ways to increase team energy through redistribution and elimination
   - Prompt: "What can we do about this? Can we redistribute draining tasks? Eliminate unnecessary ones? Automate tedious ones? Increase time on energizing work?"
   - Facilitation tip: Categories of action: (a) Eliminate - can we stop doing this entirely? (b) Automate - can a tool do this? (c) Redistribute - can someone who finds this energizing take it? (d) Timebox - can we limit exposure? (e) Pair - can we make draining work less isolating?

6. [Stage: Recharge Ideas] (5 minutes)
   - Purpose: Identify what would recharge the team proactively
   - Prompt: "What would recharge our batteries? What experiences, changes, or practices would restore energy?"
   - Facilitation tip: Keep this brief and creative. Ideas might be about team rituals (learning days, social time), individual practices (focus time, flex hours), or structural changes (fewer meetings, async communication).

7. [Synthesize] (5 minutes)
   - Summarize: key drains identified, redistribution opportunities, items to eliminate
   - Confirm 2–3 specific actions the team will take
   - Note any individual follow-ups (someone who is deeply drained may need 1:1 support)

8. [Close] (5 minutes)
   - Appreciate honesty: "Thank you for being real about what energizes and drains you"
   - Confirm actions and owners
   - Quick close: "One thing you're looking forward to next [sprint/month]"
</Workflow - Energy Levels Retrospective>
</Instructions>

<Resources>
- The most powerful insight from this retro is often the mismatch: Activity X drains Person A but energizes Person B. This creates natural redistribution opportunities that improve EVERYONE's experience without adding total effort.
- Be alert for signs that someone is deeply depleted (consistently empty, disengaged during the session itself). This may need private 1:1 follow-up with their manager, not just team-level action.
- Distinguish between "draining because it's the wrong TYPE of work for me" and "draining because there's TOO MUCH of it." The first is solved by redistribution; the second requires capacity adjustment.
- Energy data is seasonal - end-of-year is different from fresh-start-of-year. Long projects have natural energy arcs. Normalize that energy fluctuates and the goal is awareness, not constant high energy.
- Consider pairing this with the Niko-Niko calendar for ongoing mood tracking between formal energy retros. Daily mood data provides context for the quarterly energy assessment.
</Resources>
