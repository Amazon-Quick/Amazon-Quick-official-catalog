# I Like, I Wish, What If

<Metadata>
Name: I Like, I Wish, What If
Category: agile
Aliases: IL/IW/WI, Like-Wish-What If, Stanford Feedback Format
Duration: 30-45 minutes
Team Size: 3-8 (max 12)
Best For: Teams that need to generate creative ideas and experiments, not just identify problems, the "What If" element invites innovation
Definition: A constructive feedback framework from Stanford d.school using three first-person lenses, appreciating what works (Like), expressing desires for improvement (Wish), and exploring creative possibilities (What If), with the "What If" element explicitly inviting speculative innovation without requiring commitment.
</Metadata>

<Gotchas>
1. Use when: Want to generate new ideas, not just identify problems; design thinking or creative environments; teams tend toward complaint without constructive proposals; prototype or experimental sprints; innovation-focused teams or hackathon retros
2. Do NOT use when: Need highly structured process improvement with measurable outcomes; urgent operational issues requiring immediate decisive action; "What If" becomes avoidance of addressing real, known problems; teams that need concrete process fixes, not creative brainstorming
3. Dropping the first person: "The process should change" instead of "I wish our process would..." Fix: The "I" creates ownership; enforce first-person framing as non-negotiable.
4. "I Wish" becomes "You should have": Blame disguised as constructive feedback. Fix: Redirect to systemic wishes: "I wish our process would..." Keep it systemic.
5. What If as avoidance: Using creative brainstorming to avoid addressing known, fixable problems in the Wish column. Fix: Ensure Wishes get action items BEFORE selecting What If experiments.
6. Too many experiments: Selecting 5+ What Ifs overwhelms the team. Fix: Limit to 1-2 real experiments with defined timeframes and success criteria.
7. No follow-up on experiments: Running experiments without evaluating results. Fix: Always define success criteria upfront and evaluate at the next retro.
</Gotchas>

<Instructions>
<Workflow - I Like, I Wish, What If
  description="Facilitation guide for I Like, I Wish, What If retrospective."
>
1. [Setup] Prepare a board with three columns: "I Like... 💚," "I Wish... 💛," and "What If... 💡." Provide colored sticky notes for each column. Allocate time: 7 min writing, 10 min sharing, 10 min discussion, 8 min experiment selection, 5 min close.
2. [Open] (3 minutes)
   - Read the Prime Directive
   - Frame: "Today we're using first-person statements. Not 'the team should' but 'I like,' 'I wish,' 'What if.' This creates ownership and invites creativity."
   - Emphasize: "The 'What If' column is magic. It's where you get to dream without committing. Wild ideas welcome."
3. [Stage: Silent Writing] (7 minutes)
   - Purpose: Generate feedback in first-person voice across three dimensions
   - Prompt: "Complete these sentences on sticky notes. 'I LIKE...': what do you appreciate? What's working? What do you want more of? 'I WISH...': what do you desire? What would you change? What would make this better? 'WHAT IF...': what wild, creative, speculative idea could we try? What if we..."
   - Facilitation tip: The first-person framing is NON-NEGOTIABLE. It's what makes this format work. If someone writes "The team should X," gently redirect: "Can you reframe as 'I wish...'?"
4. [Stage: Share I Like] (3 minutes)
   - Purpose: Start with appreciation and positive acknowledgment
   - Prompt: "Share your 'I Like' statements. What are you appreciating?"
   - Facilitation tip: Let appreciation land. Don't rush past it; genuine appreciation builds the safety needed for honest Wishes.
5. [Stage: Share I Wish] (4 minutes)
   - Purpose: Express desires for improvement without blame
   - Prompt: "Share your 'I Wish' statements. What would you change?"
   - Facilitation tip: Watch for blame disguised as wishes: "I wish John would..." → redirect to "I wish our process would..." Keep it systemic.
6. [Stage: Share What If] (5 minutes)
   - Purpose: Explore creative possibilities and invite innovation
   - Prompt: "Share your 'What If' statements. Dream big. These are experiments, not commitments. What if we tried something completely different?"
   - Facilitation tip: This is where the energy should peak. Encourage building on each other's ideas: "Ooh, what if we combined those two What Ifs?"
7. [Stage: Select Experiments] (8 minutes)
   - Purpose: Choose 1-2 "What If" ideas to actually try
   - Prompt: "Which What If ideas have the most energy? Vote on which experiment you'd be willing to try next sprint, low risk, high learning potential."
   - Facilitation tip: Frame selected items as EXPERIMENTS with defined timeframes, not permanent changes. "We'll try this for one sprint and evaluate."
8. [Synthesize] Address top Wishes with specific actions. Select 1-2 What Ifs as time-boxed experiments. Document Likes as team strengths.
9. [Close] (3 minutes)
   - Read experiments selected and Wish-based actions
   - Ask: "What's one What If you hope we try someday, even if not now?"
   - Thank the team for creative thinking
</Workflow - I Like, I Wish, What If>
</Instructions>

<Resources>
- First-person framing is the CORE MECHANIC; enforce it. It transforms blame into ownership and creates psychological safety
- "What If" is where INNOVATION lives in retros; most formats only identify problems. This one generates solutions
- Separate critique (Wish) from possibility (What If). They serve different purposes
- For teams that only complain: mandate that every Wish must be paired with a What If: "You've identified what's wrong, now dream about what could be"
- This format originated in design critiques. It's perfect for product teams, UX teams, and innovation sprints
- For remote teams, use anonymous submission for "I Wish" items to increase honesty
- The "Someday/Maybe" list becomes a team innovation backlog. Review it quarterly
</Resources>
