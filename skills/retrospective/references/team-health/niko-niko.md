# Niko-Niko Calendar

<Metadata>
Name: Niko-Niko Calendar
Category: team-health
Aliases: Mood Calendar, Happiness Index, Daily Mood Check, Smiley Calendar
Duration: 30 seconds daily + 15–30 minutes periodic review
Team Size: 3–15
Best For: Longitudinal daily mood tracking that reveals patterns and correlations between team morale and events over time
Definition: A daily mood tracking practice (not a single meeting) where team members record their emotional state each day using simple indicators (smileys, colors).
</Metadata>

<Gotchas>
1. Use when: As an ongoing daily practice (not a one-time event); When you want longitudinal mood data correlated with team events; To identify which activities, decisions, or external factors affect team mood; As input to other retrospectives (provides data that memory can't); When a team appears stressed but you lack concrete data
2. Do NOT use when: As a one-off assessment (use Energy Levels retro instead); In low-trust environments where people won't be honest about mood; When daily recording becomes coercive or performative; When management will use mood data punitively
3. Manager surveillance: Using mood data to monitor individual performance or identify "negative" team members. Fix: Mood data is team-owned, for team improvement. Never used in performance reviews or individual assessments.
4. Peer pressure to be happy: Social pressure to always show green, making the data meaningless. Fix: Normalize non-green days. Leaders should model honest recording including their own bad days.
5. Data without action: Collecting mood data religiously but never reviewing or acting on patterns. Fix: Schedule regular reviews. If you're not going to act on it, stop collecting it.
6. Mandatory participation: Forcing people to record daily, creating resentment. Fix: Keep it voluntary. If participation drops, ask why (maybe the format needs changing, not enforcement).
7. Over-interpretation: Reading too much into a single bad day. Fix: Look for patterns over weeks, not individual entries. One bad day is noise; five consecutive bad days is signal.
</Gotchas>

<Instructions>
<Workflow - Niko-Niko Calendar
  description="Step-by-step facilitation guide for Niko-Niko Calendar."
>
1. [Setup] (One-time; 10 minutes)
   - Create the calendar: physical (poster/whiteboard) or digital (shared spreadsheet, Slack bot, or dedicated tool)
   - Format: Grid with team members as rows, days as columns
   - Define the scale (keep it simple): 😄 Great, 😐 Okay, 😟 Not good (3 levels is enough)
   - Establish the norm: record at end of each work day, takes < 30 seconds
   - Clarify: recording is voluntary, private reflection is sufficient, comments optional
   - Set review cadence: weekly or at each retrospective

2. [Open; Daily Recording] (30 seconds each day)
   - Each person records their mood at end of day
   - No explanation required; just the indicator
   - Optional: brief comment for personal reference ("deadline stress", "great pairing session")
   - Facilitator doesn't probe individual daily entries

3. [Stage: Periodic Review; Data Presentation] (5 minutes)
   - Purpose: Visualize patterns in team mood over the review period
   - Prompt: "Let's look at our mood data for the past [1-2 weeks]. What patterns do you see?"
   - Facilitation tip: Display the calendar visually. Look for: clusters of bad days (what happened that week?), consistent bad days for individuals (something structural?), good-day patterns (what enables those?). Present the data without interpretation first.

4. [Stage: Periodic Review; Correlation Analysis] (10 minutes)
   - Purpose: Connect mood patterns to specific events, decisions, or circumstances
   - Prompt: "We see [pattern X] in our mood data. What was happening during that period? What correlates with our best days? Our worst?"
   - Facilitation tip: Overlay events on the calendar: deadlines, meetings, releases, holidays, team changes. Look for correlations. "Every Monday is yellow/red for most people" might indicate meeting-heavy Mondays. "Days after releases are green" might indicate the relief of delivery.

5. [Stage: Periodic Review; Action Items] (10 minutes)
   - Purpose: Take action based on mood data to improve team experience
   - Prompt: "Based on these patterns - what can we change? Can we reduce mood drains? Can we create more conditions for good days?"
   - Facilitation tip: Actions might be structural (move a draining meeting, protect focus time on identified bad days), relational (check in with someone who's consistently low), or celebratory (acknowledge what creates good days and do more of it).

6. [Synthesize] (5 minutes)
   - Note key patterns identified
   - Confirm actions from this review
   - Reset or continue the calendar for the next period

7. [Close] (2 minutes)
   - Thank team for consistent recording
   - Remind of the purpose: "This data helps us be intentional about team wellness"
   - Confirm next review date
</Workflow - Niko-Niko Calendar>
</Instructions>

<Resources>
- Keep daily recording EFFORTLESS - if it takes more than 30 seconds, participation will drop. A single emoji or color selection is ideal. Don't require explanations.
- Never pressure people to explain a bad mood publicly. The pattern matters, not the individual entry. If someone is consistently low, follow up PRIVATELY.
- The most valuable insight comes from correlating mood with events. Maintain a simple "event log" alongside the mood calendar: releases, deadlines, big meetings, team changes. The overlap tells the story.
- For remote/distributed teams, automated end-of-day prompts (Slack bot, email reminder) increase consistency. Make it frictionless.
- Consider aggregating to team level for group reviews: "70% of the team reported Great on Thursday" vs. individual scrutiny. This protects privacy while enabling pattern discussion.
- Ideal review cadence is every 2 weeks or at each sprint retrospective. Weekly reviews are too frequent (not enough data); monthly reviews miss timely interventions.
</Resources>
