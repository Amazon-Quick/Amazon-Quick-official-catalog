# Trust Battery Retrospective

<Metadata>
Name: Trust Battery Retrospective
Category: team-health
Aliases: Trust Level Check, Relationship Retro, Trust Assessment
Duration: 60–90 minutes
Team Size: 3–10
Best For: Assessing and rebuilding interpersonal trust within a team using the "battery" metaphor to make abstract trust levels concrete and actionable
Definition: A concept from Tobi Lütke (CEO, Shopify) where team members assess their "trust battery" level with the team and discuss what charges or drains trust.
</Metadata>

<Gotchas>
1. Use when: After team conflicts that damaged working relationships; When collaboration is suffering and people seem guarded; During new team formation (setting expectations for trust-building); When "something feels off" but it's hard to articulate; After organizational changes that disrupted established relationships; When a team has been together long enough to have trust history (3+ months)
2. Do NOT use when: Zero psychological safety exists (the retro would backfire; build safety first); Very large teams (too interpersonal for groups above 10); When specific individuals are the problem and need 1:1 intervention rather than group discussion; As a punitive exercise ("your trust battery is low ;  explain yourself"); When active HR investigations are underway
3. Weaponizing trust scores: Using trust battery levels as ammunition ("Your battery is at 30%!") to attack or shame. Fix: Numbers are for self-awareness, not for wielding against others. If someone shares a low number, respond with curiosity, not defensiveness.
4. All talk, no follow-through: Having a meaningful trust conversation but never following up on commitments. Fix: Schedule an explicit follow-up. Trust is built through consistent action, not one-time discussions.
5. Forced openness too soon: Pushing people into open trust discussion before safety exists. Fix: Start anonymous. Build toward openness over multiple sessions. Read the room; if people are guarded, respect that and work at the edge of comfort, not beyond it.
6. Person-focused blame: Turning trust drains into character assassination of specific team members. Fix: Maintain behavioral framing relentlessly. "When commitments aren't kept, my trust drains" not "John never follows through."
7. Skipping the positive: Jumping straight to trust drains without first establishing what charges trust. Fix: Always start with trust chargers. This creates safety and provides the behavioral vocabulary for what "good" looks like.
</Gotchas>

<Instructions>
<Workflow - Trust Battery Retrospective
  description="Step-by-step facilitation guide for Trust Battery Retrospective."
>
1. [Setup] (3–5 days prior)
   - Assess whether psychological safety is sufficient for this format (if unsure, do a Psychological Safety retro first)
   - Optionally send anonymous pre-survey: "On a scale of 0-100%, where is your trust battery with the team overall?"
   - Secure a neutral facilitator (not direct manager for the first time)
   - Prepare the space: private, comfortable, sufficient time (don't rush trust conversations)
   - Brief participants: "We'll be discussing trust dynamics. Come prepared with an open mind and willingness to be honest."

2. [Open] (10 minutes)
   - Explain the Trust Battery concept: "Every relationship has a trust battery. It starts at 50% when you first meet someone. Every interaction either charges or drains it. Today we'll make our trust dynamics visible."
   - Set ground rules: Focus on behaviors (not character), own your statements (I-language), commitment to action (not just venting), confidentiality
   - Facilitator models first: "My trust battery with this group is at X% because [honest sharing]"
   - Acknowledge vulnerability: "This conversation takes courage. Thank you for being here."

3. [Stage: Trust Level Check-In] (10 minutes)
   - Purpose: Establish where trust currently sits - making the invisible visible
   - Prompt: "Where is your trust battery with the team overall right now? 0% to 100%. You can share a number, or describe it in words: 'fully charged,' 'about half,' 'running low.'"
   - Facilitation tip: Can be anonymous (write on paper, facilitator reads) or open (round-robin). Anonymous is safer for first attempts. Don't require explanations at this stage; just the data point. Aggregate for the team: "Our average is X%, ranging from Y to Z."

4. [Stage: What Charges Trust] (20 minutes)
   - Purpose: Identify specific behaviors and patterns that build trust on this team
   - Prompt: "Think of a time when your trust battery charged - when someone did something that made you trust them or the team more. What was it? What specific behaviors charge trust for you?"
   - Facilitation tip: Start with positive. This celebrates what's working and provides a menu of trust-building behaviors. Capture specifics: "Following through on commitments" is better than "being reliable." "Admitting 'I don't know'" is better than "being honest." Build a visible list of trust-chargers.

5. [Stage: What Drains Trust] (20 minutes)
   - Purpose: Surface behaviors and patterns that erode trust - without blame
   - Prompt: "What drains trust for you? What behaviors or patterns, when they happen, reduce your trust battery? Keep this about behaviors, not people."
   - Facilitation tip: This is the difficult stage. Maintain blameless framing. If someone gets specific about a person, redirect: "Can we describe the behavior pattern rather than attributing it?" Common drains: broken commitments, gossip, exclusion from decisions, inconsistency between words and actions. If tension rises, normalize it: "This is hard to talk about; that's precisely why it matters."

6. [Stage: Personal Commitments] (15 minutes)
   - Purpose: Each person commits to specific trust-charging behaviors
   - Prompt: "Based on what you've heard about what charges and drains trust for others - what will YOU personally commit to doing more of? What will you commit to doing less of?"
   - Facilitation tip: Round-robin: each person makes 1–2 specific behavioral commitments. These should be observable by others. "I commit to responding to messages within 24 hours" not "I'll try to communicate better." Write them down publicly. This creates accountability.

7. [Synthesize] (10 minutes)
   - Read back the trust-charging behaviors list (team reference)
   - Read back individual commitments
   - Confirm follow-up plan: when will we check in on these commitments?
   - Note anything that needs private follow-up

8. [Close] (10 minutes)
   - Acknowledge the vulnerability required: "What happened in this room took real courage"
   - Quick round: "One commitment you heard from someone else that meant something to you"
   - Confirm confidentiality: specific shares stay in the room; general agreements are public
   - Schedule follow-up check-in (4–6 weeks)
   - Remind: "Trust is built in small moments over time - not in one session"
</Workflow - Trust Battery Retrospective>
</Instructions>

<Resources>
- This is one of the most vulnerable retrospective formats. Only run it when psychological safety is moderate-to-high. If safety is low, run a Psychological Safety retro first to build the foundation.
- The facilitator MUST model vulnerability first. Share your own trust battery level honestly. Show that it's okay to be at 60%; that's useful data, not failure.
- For first attempts, consider fully anonymous input (digital sticky notes) for the "drains" section. People need practice being honest about trust dynamics before doing it face-to-face.
- If specific interpersonal issues surface that need resolution, note them for private follow-up rather than resolving them in the group session. The group session is for patterns; the 1:1 is for specific relationships.
- The follow-up is where trust is actually built. The session creates awareness; the daily behavior creates change. Make the follow-up check-in non-negotiable.
- Consider pairing with a Working Agreements Review; trust dynamics often point to implicit expectations that should be made explicit.
</Resources>
