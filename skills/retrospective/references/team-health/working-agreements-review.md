# Working Agreements Review

<Metadata>
Name: Working Agreements Review
Category: team-health
Aliases: Team Norms Review, Social Contract Retro, Ground Rules Retro
Duration: 30–60 minutes
Team Size: 3–12
Best For: Reviewing and updating the team's explicit norms and working agreements to ensure they remain relevant, followed, and genuinely shared
Definition: A retrospective specifically focused on reviewing and updating the team's working agreements; the explicit norms, rules, and expectations the team has set for how they work together.
</Metadata>

<Gotchas>
1. Use when: When new team members join (onboarding into the social contract); After conflicts about expectations (the agreement was unclear or violated); Quarterly maintenance to keep agreements fresh; When the team is forming or re-forming after significant change; When agreements feel stale, violated, or irrelevant; After a Working From Home/Return-to-Office policy change
2. Do NOT use when: Every sprint (too frequent for this topic; norms don't need weekly review); When the team has bigger issues than norms (address the crisis first); When power dynamics prevent genuine agreement (if people can't say "no," agreements are imposed, not shared); When no working agreements exist yet (create them first in a different session)
3. Agreements imposed by management: Manager writes working agreements and announces them to the team. Fix: Agreements must be CO-CREATED. Everyone affected must genuinely agree. If the team didn't write it together, it's a policy, not an agreement.
4. Wallpaper agreements: Agreements that are written once and never referenced, reviewed, or enforced. Fix: Reference them in the moment when relevant. Review quarterly minimum. If violated, address it kindly but promptly.
5. Too many agreements: List of 15+ agreements that no one can remember. Fix: Maximum 5–8 agreements. If you need more, you might need a process document instead.
6. Vague agreements: "We communicate well" or "We respect each other." Fix: Every agreement must be observable; you should be able to point to a moment and say "that was following our agreement" or "that violated it."
7. Forced consensus: People agree because they feel pressured, not because they genuinely commit. Fix: Explicitly invite dissent: "Does anyone have a concern about this? It's okay to push back." Test: "Can you commit to this for the next quarter?"
</Gotchas>

<Instructions>
<Workflow - Working Agreements Review
  description="Step-by-step facilitation guide for Working Agreements Review."
>
1. [Setup] (Day before)
   - Print or display current working agreements document
   - Note when agreements were last reviewed
   - If new members have joined since last review, flag this
   - Prepare "agreement cards" - each current agreement on a separate card for sorting

2. [Open] (5 minutes)
   - State purpose: "Our working agreements are our social contract. Today we're checking: Are they still relevant? Are we following them? Do we need new ones?"
   - Read each existing agreement aloud (reconnect with what was agreed)
   - Reminder: "Agreements only work if everyone genuinely agrees - not if they're imposed. You have full permission to challenge any agreement here."

3. [Stage: Review Existing Agreements] (15 minutes)
   - Purpose: Assess each current agreement for relevance, adherence, and value
   - Prompt: "For each agreement, let's ask three questions: (1) Is it still relevant? (2) Are we actually following it? (3) Is it helping us?"
   - Facilitation tip: For each agreement, vote: Keep (still working) / Drop (no longer relevant) / Modify (needs updating). Be honest about adherence; if no one is following it, either recommit or drop it. A dead agreement is worse than no agreement (it teaches the team that agreements don't matter).

4. [Stage: Identify Gaps] (10 minutes)
   - Purpose: Surface situations where the lack of an explicit agreement caused friction
   - Prompt: "Have there been any situations recently where unclear expectations caused frustration? Where do we need a new agreement that doesn't exist yet?"
   - Facilitation tip: Look for: repeated misunderstandings, passive-aggressive friction, "I assumed you would..." moments. These indicate implicit expectations that should be made explicit. Common gap areas: response time expectations, meeting camera norms, async communication protocols, decision-making authority.

5. [Stage: Propose New Agreements] (10 minutes)
   - Purpose: Create new agreements for identified gaps
   - Prompt: "For each gap identified - what agreement would prevent this friction in the future? Remember: good agreements are specific, observable, and genuinely shared."
   - Facilitation tip: Good agreements are: (a) Specific: "We respond to Slack messages within 4 business hours" not "We communicate promptly." (b) Observable: You can tell whether it's being followed. (c) Shared: Everyone genuinely agrees, not just the loudest voice. Test: "Can everyone live with this? Speak now if not."

6. [Stage: Commitment] (10 minutes)
   - Purpose: Recommit to updated agreements - make them real, not wallpaper
   - Prompt: "Here's our updated set of agreements. Does everyone genuinely commit to these? Not 'I guess so' - genuinely 'yes, I will follow this.' If any feel wrong, speak now."
   - Facilitation tip: Go around the room: each person verbally commits. This creates social accountability. If someone hesitates, explore why; it's better to modify the agreement than to have uncommitted compliance. Post agreements somewhere visible and reference them when relevant.

7. [Synthesize] (5 minutes)
   - Read the final updated agreement set
   - Note what was dropped, modified, and added
   - Confirm where agreements will be posted/stored
   - Set next review date

8. [Close] (5 minutes)
   - Thank team for honest engagement
   - Remind: "These are living agreements; if something isn't working, raise it. Don't wait for the next review."
   - Confirm next review date (typically quarterly or when team changes)
</Workflow - Working Agreements Review>
</Instructions>

<Resources>
- The most common problem with working agreements is that they become wallpaper; written once, never referenced. The review retro itself is the mechanism that keeps them alive. Establish consistent cadence.
- When new team members join, this review is essential. They need to: (a) understand the existing agreements, (b) have the opportunity to negotiate/question, and (c) explicitly commit. Don't just hand them a document.
- Good agreements typically cover: communication expectations (response times, channels), meeting norms (cameras, punctuality, preparation), decision-making approach (consensus, delegation, veto), and work boundaries (availability, focus time protection).
- If an agreement is consistently violated without consequence, it's worse than not having it; it teaches the team that agreements are meaningless. Either recommit and enforce, or honestly drop it.
- Fewer is better. A team with 5 clear, followed agreements outperforms a team with 15 aspirational ones. Each agreement should earn its place by preventing real friction.
</Resources>
