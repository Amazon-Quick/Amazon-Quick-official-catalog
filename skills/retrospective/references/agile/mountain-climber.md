# Mountain Climber

<Metadata>
Name: Mountain Climber
Category: agile
Aliases: Summit Retro, Climbing Retrospective, Peak Perspective
Duration: 45-60 minutes
Team Size: 4-8 (max 12)
Best For: End-of-milestone celebrations that help teams appreciate progress and prepare for the next challenge
Definition: A retrospective that uses the metaphor of climbing a mountain to help teams gain perspective on their journey, looking back at what they achieved, what equipment helped, what obstacles they overcame, and what they need for the next peak.
</Metadata>

<Gotchas>
1. Use when: End of a significant milestone or project phase; team feels stuck and needs perspective on how far they've come; celebration and acknowledgment are needed; long-running projects where progress feels invisible; after shipping a major feature or completing a difficult quarter
2. Do NOT use when: Mid-sprint quick reflection (too ambitious for short check-ins); team genuinely hasn't achieved much (forced celebration feels hollow); urgent problem-solving needed; team needs to address immediate blockers, not reflect
3. Rushing past celebration: Jumping immediately to "next peak" without honoring the summit. Fix: The whole point is PERSPECTIVE first. Give Summit View generous time before planning.
4. Forced celebration: When the team genuinely struggled and didn't achieve much, this format feels dishonest. Fix: Only use after real progress. Pick a different format otherwise.
5. Ignoring the obstacles: Pretending the climb was easy dishonors the effort. Fix: Acknowledge the hard parts explicitly. Say "We overcame THIS, and we can overcome what's next."
6. No forward look: Getting stuck in celebration without planning for what's next. Fix: Always include the Next Peak stage with concrete preparation actions.
7. Base camp amnesia: Forgetting where the team started. Fix: Explicitly mark "where we were" vs "where we are" to make progress visible.
</Gotchas>

<Instructions>
<Workflow - Mountain Climber
  description="Facilitation guide for Mountain Climber retrospective."
>
1. [Setup] Draw a mountain on the board with: the summit (top), climbing gear (side), obstacles along the path, and the next peak visible in the distance. Mark "Base Camp" at the bottom and "You Are Here" at the summit. Allocate time: 5 min framing, 7 min reflection, 15 min sharing, 10 min planning, 5 min close.
2. [Open] (5 minutes)
   - Read the Prime Directive
   - Frame: "Congratulations. We've reached a summit. Before we head for the next peak, let's take a moment to look back at the climb. What got us here? What did we overcome? And what do we need for the next ascent?"
   - Set the tone: This is a CELEBRATION first, planning second.
3. [Stage: Summit View] (7 minutes)
   - Purpose: Appreciate achievements and recognize how far the team has come
   - Prompt: "Look down from the summit. What did we achieve? What can you see from up here that you couldn't see at base camp? How far have we come? What are you proud of?"
   - Facilitation tip: Give this stage generous time. Teams rarely take time to acknowledge their own progress. Push for specifics: "What's different now compared to when we started?"
4. [Stage: Climbing Gear] (5 minutes)
   - Purpose: Identify the tools, practices, and people that enabled success
   - Prompt: "What was our climbing gear? What tools, processes, practices, or support systems helped us reach the summit? What would we not have made it without?"
   - Facilitation tip: This surfaces the team's strengths and successful practices to preserve.
5. [Stage: Obstacles Overcome] (5 minutes)
   - Purpose: Acknowledge difficulties without dwelling on them
   - Prompt: "What obstacles did we climb over, around, or through? What was the hardest part of the ascent? What nearly stopped us?"
   - Facilitation tip: Frame as PAST challenges successfully navigated. This builds confidence. "We overcame THIS, so we can overcome what's next."
6. [Stage: Next Peak] (10 minutes)
   - Purpose: Look ahead and prepare for the next challenge
   - Prompt: "What's the next peak we're aiming for? What do we see ahead? What gear do we need that we don't have? What lessons from this climb will we carry forward?"
   - Facilitation tip: Connect lessons learned (gear + obstacles) to next peak preparation. Ask: "What from this climb prepares us for that one?"
7. [Synthesize] Document achievements (summit view), preserve successful practices (gear), and create a preparation plan for the next peak.
8. [Close] (5 minutes)
   - Celebrate: "We made it. That was hard, and we did it."
   - Read the preparation plan for next peak
   - Ask: "What's one word that describes this climb?"
   - Thank the team for the journey together
</Workflow - Mountain Climber>
</Instructions>

<Resources>
- This format is PERFECT for end-of-quarter, end-of-project, or major release celebrations
- The "looking down" perspective is psychologically powerful. It combats the negativity bias that makes teams forget their progress
- Always celebrate BEFORE discussing next challenges. The order matters for morale
- For remote teams, create a visual mountain in a shared doc that accumulates achievements over time
- Consider having team members physically stand up for the "summit view" stage. Embodied metaphors work
- Pair this with a team lunch or social activity for maximum morale impact
- Revisit the "climbing gear" list during planning. These are your team's proven strengths
</Resources>
