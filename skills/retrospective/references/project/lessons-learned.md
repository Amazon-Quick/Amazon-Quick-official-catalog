# Lessons Learned Session

<Metadata>
Name: Lessons Learned Session
Category: project
Aliases: Lessons Learned Meeting, Project Debrief, Knowledge Harvest, Experience Report
Duration: 60–180 minutes (depending on project size)
Team Size: 6–25
Best For: Capturing organizational knowledge at the end of a project or major phase to improve future execution
Definition: A structured session at the end of a project (or project phase) where the team identifies, documents, and analyzes what went well and what could be improved for future projects.
</Metadata>

<Gotchas>
1. Use when: End of projects or major project phases; After significant milestones where knowledge should be harvested; When transitioning between project phases (e.g., design → build); Organizational learning initiatives across a portfolio of projects; When repeating project types and wanting to avoid recurring mistakes
2. Do NOT use when: Within sprints or iterations (use sprint retros instead); When the project team has already fully disbanded and moved on; For ongoing operations without discrete project boundaries; When there's no mechanism to store and retrieve lessons (effort is wasted)
3. Write and forget: Conducting lessons learned but never consulting them for future projects. The document sits in a folder unread. Fix: Integrate retrieval into project kickoff processes.
4. End-only capture: Waiting until project end when details are forgotten and team is exhausted. Fix: Capture lessons at each phase gate, not just at project close.
5. Blame storm: Degenerating into finger-pointing about who caused problems. Fix: Read Prime Directive, focus on systems and processes not individuals.
6. Vague lessons: Recording generalities like "communicate better" without specifics. Fix: Require context + action + result for every lesson.
7. Skip the stakeholders: Only including the delivery team. Fix: Sponsors, customers, and adjacent teams have critical perspective.
</Gotchas>

<Instructions>
<Workflow - Lessons Learned Session
  description="Step-by-step facilitation guide for Lessons Learned Session."
>
1. [Setup] (Before session, 1–2 days prior)
   - Gather project data: budget actuals vs. plan, timeline adherence, quality metrics, stakeholder satisfaction scores
   - Prepare a one-page project summary with key milestones and outcomes
   - Send pre-work to participants: "Come prepared with 2–3 things that went well and 2–3 things to improve"
   - Book room/virtual space; set up whiteboard or digital board with five sections: Identify, Document, Analyze, Store, Retrieve
   - Invite all stakeholders including sponsors, delivery team, and cross-project participants

2. [Open] (10 minutes)
   - Read the Retrospective Prime Directive
   - State purpose: "We're here to learn from this project so future teams benefit; this is not a blame session"
   - Review the project summary: goals, timeline, key outcomes, team composition
   - Set ground rules: blameless, evidence-based, forward-looking

3. [Stage: Identify] (25 minutes)
   - Purpose: Surface the raw experiences, both successes and challenges
   - Prompt: "Thinking about the full project lifecycle, planning through delivery, what went well that we should repeat, and what should we improve next time?"
   - Facilitation tip: Use silent writing first (7 minutes) to prevent anchoring. Have participants write one insight per sticky note. Then share in round-robin, clustering similar items on the board.

4. [Stage: Document] (20 minutes)
   - Purpose: Add context, impact, and specificity to each lesson
   - Prompt: "For each key item, let's capture: What happened? What was the impact? What's the recommendation for future teams?"
   - Facilitation tip: Assign a dedicated note-taker. For each lesson, ensure you have the context (situation), the action taken (or not taken), and the result. Vague lessons ("communicate better") are useless. Push for specifics.

5. [Stage: Analyze] (30 minutes)
   - Purpose: Determine root causes and patterns across lessons
   - Prompt: "Looking at our clustered lessons, what patterns do you see? What root causes appear repeatedly?"
   - Facilitation tip: Use 5 Whys on the top 3–5 issues. Look for systemic patterns rather than one-off events. Connect related lessons across categories (e.g., a communication failure that caused a timeline slip).

6. [Stage: Prioritize & Recommend] (20 minutes)
   - Purpose: Convert lessons into actionable recommendations for future projects
   - Prompt: "If you could give the next project team starting a similar initiative three pieces of advice, what would they be?"
   - Facilitation tip: Dot-vote on the most impactful lessons. Convert top-voted items into specific, actionable recommendations with clear ownership for implementation.

7. [Synthesize] (15 minutes)
   - Facilitator reads back the top 5–7 lessons with their recommendations
   - Assign owners for entering lessons into the organizational knowledge repository
   - Identify which lessons should be communicated to which audiences (PMO, future teams, leadership)

8. [Close] (10 minutes)
   - Thank participants for their honesty and contributions
   - Confirm next steps: who publishes the lessons learned document, when, and where
   - Quick round: "What's one thing you'll personally carry forward from this project?"
   - Schedule any follow-up if needed (e.g., presenting to PMO)
</Workflow - Lessons Learned Session>
</Instructions>

<Resources>
- Bring hard data to anchor the conversation: budget vs. actual, timeline adherence, defect rates. Data prevents arguments about what "really" happened.
- Conduct mini lessons-learned at phase gates throughout the project (not just at the end). This reduces the recency bias problem and captures insights while fresh.
- If the project failed, extra attention to psychological safety is needed. Consider having a neutral third party facilitate.
- For large projects (20+ participants), use a "fishbowl" format: inner circle discusses while outer circle listens, then rotate.
- Always close with appreciation. Acknowledge that the team's willingness to be honest is itself a success that benefits the organization.
</Resources>
